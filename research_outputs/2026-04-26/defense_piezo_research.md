# U.S. Department of Defense and Adjacent Agency Funding for Piezo Channel, Mechanosensitive Ion Channel, Sonogenetics, Focused-Ultrasound Neuromodulation, and RF-Induced Neural Effects Research (2015–2026)

**Prepared:** April 26, 2026  
**Classification:** Unclassified — based entirely on publicly available sources  
**Scope:** DoD agencies and adjacent federal agencies; awards/programs from 2015 onward

---

## Executive Summary

This report investigates whether research into mechanosensitive ion channels (principally Piezo1 and Piezo2), sonogenetics, focused-ultrasound (FUS) neuromodulation, and radiofrequency (RF)-induced neural effects has been funded by the U.S. Department of Defense or adjacent federal agencies since 2015, and whether such research has been operationalized in defense contexts.

**Key finding:** Yes, there is a clear and documented lineage of DoD-funded research that has both implicitly and explicitly engaged mechanosensitive channel biology in defense-relevant applications.

**The strongest single data point** is DARPA contract **HR0011-15-2-0054** (ElectRx program, Columbia University, ~$3.33M, 2015–2019), which directly funded research that culminated in the 2022 *PNAS* paper explicitly demonstrating that **PIEZO2 is the mechanically gated ion channel responsible for focused-ultrasound activation of peripheral sensory neurons** — the first peer-reviewed demonstration of a named Piezo channel mediating a DARPA-funded neuromodulation mechanism.

Beyond that single anchor point, the research landscape shows:

1. **DARPA N3 program** (~$104M total, 2019–2023): Three of six performers (Teledyne, PARC, Rice MOANA) use focused ultrasound as a neural "write" mechanism. None of the public N3 abstracts mention Piezo channels by name, but the ultrasound-write mechanisms they employ implicitly depend on mechanosensitive transduction. AFRL (711HPW) personnel appear as human-demonstration collaborators on CMU's N3 team.

2. **AFRL 711th Human Performance Wing / RF Bioeffects Branch** (JBSA Fort Sam Houston): An active, continuously funded ($3.6M+ in active grants as of 2024) research program on RF electromagnetic field effects on hippocampal neurons and transcranial neuromodulation via 2.45 GHz microwave (TWITCH program). No explicit Piezo-channel language appears in public materials; mechanism is attributed to voltage-gated calcium channels.

3. **ARPA-H sonogenetics award** ($41.3M, 2026, Salk Institute/Chalasani): The single largest federal investment in sonogenetics. ARPA-H is an HHS agency, not DoD, but it is modeled on DARPA's mission structure.

4. **No confirmed DoD SBIR/STTR awards** explicitly naming Piezo channels, mechanosensitive channels, or sonogenetics were found in public records. SBIR.gov direct searches failed due to web access restrictions; this absence should be treated as a data gap, not a confirmed negative.

5. **No DTRA, IARPA, WRAIR post-2015, JNLWD, DOE lab, or NSWC Crane** programs explicitly targeting Piezo/mechanosensitive channels or sonogenetics for neural effects were found in public record.

The overall picture is one of **implicit engagement** (DoD funds FUS neuromodulation that mechanistically depends on Piezo channels, but program documents do not say "Piezo") layered over **one explicit engagement** (the Columbia DARPA ElectRx work). There is no evidence of a covert or classified program specifically targeting Piezo/mechanosensitive channels for offensive or non-lethal weapons purposes in the public record — though the existence of classified work in this space cannot be ruled out.

## Detailed Program Write-Ups

### 1. DARPA ElectRx — Columbia University / Konofagou (MOST RELEVANT)

**Agency:** DARPA, Biological Technologies Office  
**Contract:** HR0011-15-2-0054  
**Recipient:** Columbia University, Department of Biomedical Engineering  
**PI:** Elisa Konofagou, PhD (Robert and Margaret Hariri Professor of Biomedical Engineering)  
**Period:** 2015–2019 (4-year grant)  
**Amount:** ~$3.33M  
**Program context:** ElectRx total = $78.9M to 7 teams; Doug Weber, Program Manager  
**URL:** [DARPA ElectRx page](https://www.darpa.mil/research/programs/electrical-prescriptions) | [Columbia BME announcement](https://www.bme.columbia.edu/news/elisa-konofagou%E2%80%99s-new-darpa-grant-advances-work-focused-ultrasound) | [Poster citing contract number](https://brainbox-initiative.com/cms-data/posters/Stephen%20lee%20poster.pdf)

**Scope:** The Columbia team used focused ultrasound (FUS) to noninvasively stimulate peripheral nerves to modulate autonomic organ function — a proof-of-concept for bioelectronic medicine as a treatment for warfighter conditions (pain, inflammation, PTSD, anxiety). Konofagou described the mechanism in a public statement: *"it exerts mechanical pressure on that tissue, which **stimulates specific mechanosensitive channels in neurons** and causes them to 'turn on.'"* Collaborators included Ellen Lumpkin (somatosensory neurobiology, Columbia) and Ken Shepard (electrical engineering, Columbia).

**Piezo/mechanosensitive relevance — DIRECT AND CONFIRMED:**  
This contract directly funded the research that produced the landmark 2022 *PNAS* paper:

> Hoffman BU, Baba Y, Lee SA, Tong CK, Konofagou EE, Lumpkin EA. "Focused ultrasound excites action potentials in mammalian peripheral neurons in part through the mechanically gated ion channel PIEZO2." *Proc Natl Acad Sci USA.* 2022;119(21):e2115821119.  
> **Funded by DARPA HR0011-15-2-0054 + NIH R01EB027576.**  
> Source: [PMC9173751](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9173751/)

**Key finding of that paper:** PIEZO2 is the mechanically gated ion channel at the sensory neuron membrane that sets the threshold for FUS-induced action potentials in peripheral tissues. Blocking PIEZO2 pharmacologically or genetically reduces FUS-evoked neural responses. This is the first peer-reviewed demonstration that a named Piezo channel directly mediates a DARPA-funded FUS neuromodulation mechanism.

**Significance for defense context:** This work establishes the mechanistic basis for using FUS to selectively activate peripheral sensory neurons — a capability with dual-use potential for pain control, inflammation modulation, and potentially targeted sensory manipulation.

**Honesty note:** The DARPA contract funded the FUS neuromodulation work; the PI did not set out specifically to study Piezo2. The Piezo2 finding was a mechanistic discovery that emerged from the research. There is no indication DARPA directed Konofagou to study Piezo channels specifically.

### 2. DARPA ElectRx — UC Berkeley "Neural Dust" (Piezoelectric Transducers)

**Agency:** DARPA, Biological Technologies Office  
**Recipient:** UC Berkeley, Department of Electrical Engineering and Computer Sciences  
**PIs:** Michel Maharbiz, Jose Carmena, Dongjin Seo  
**Period:** 2015–2017+ (Phase 1 of ElectRx)  
**Amount:** Not publicly disclosed  
**URLs:** [DARPA announcement](https://www.darpa.mil/news/2016/implantable-neural-dust) | [Berkeley News](https://news.berkeley.edu/2016/08/03/sprinkling-of-neural-dust-opens-door-to-electroceuticals/)

**Scope:** Developed millimeter-scale, batteryless wireless implants ("neural dust motes") for peripheral nerve and muscle recording and stimulation. Each mote contains:
- A pair of electrodes to measure nerve signals
- A custom transistor
- **A piezoelectric crystal** that converts external ultrasound pulses into electrical power and communicates recorded signals back via ultrasound backscatter

**Piezo relevance:** The *technology* uses piezoelectric crystals (PZT, not Piezo ion channels) as transducers. This is a materials-science use of piezoelectricity, not a biological Piezo1/2 channel application. However, the system uses FUS as both a power source and a neural-read mechanism. The paper was published in *Neuron* (Seo et al., 2016).

DARPA program manager Doug Weber confirmed this was a Phase 1 ElectRx proof-of-concept, stating: *"By using ultrasound to communicate with the neural dust, the sensors can be made smaller and placed deeper inside the body, by needle injection or other non-surgical approaches."* [Source: DARPA.mil](https://www.darpa.mil/news/2016/implantable-neural-dust)

### 3. DARPA N3 — Full Program Overview

**Agency:** DARPA, Microsystems Technology Office (MTO)  
**BAA:** HR001118S0029  
**Period:** 2019–2023 (~4 years)  
**Total budget:** ~$104M+ across 6 performers  
**URLs:** [DARPA N3 program page](https://www.darpa.mil/research/programs/next-generation-nonsurgical-neurotechnology) | [2019 announcement](https://www.darpa.mil/news/2019/nonsurgical-brain-machine-interfaces)

**Program goal:** Develop high-performance, bi-directional brain-machine interfaces for **able-bodied service members** — with the precision to read from and write to 16 independent channels within a 16 mm³ volume of neural tissue within 50 ms, without surgery.

**Six performers and their FUS/mechanosensitive relevance:**

**A. Teledyne Scientific (MINDSS) — Contract N6523619C8013**  
PI: Patrick Connolly | Award: $12.8M (fully obligated) | Source: [HigherGov](https://www.highergov.com/contract/N6523619C8013/)  
Approach: Optically pumped magnetometers for neural reading + **focused ultrasound for neural writing**. FUS as the write mechanism implies mechanosensitive transduction but no public abstract mentions Piezo channels.

**B. Palo Alto Research Center (PARC) — Contract N6523619C8014**  
PI: Krishnan Thyagarajan | Award: Up to $14.2M potential ($2.3M obligated) | Source: [HigherGov](https://www.highergov.com/contract/N6523619C8014/)  
Approach: Acousto-magnetic stimulation (combined ultrasound + magnetic fields) for noninvasive neural writing.

**C. Rice University (MOANA)**  
PI: Jacob Robinson | Award: $18M initial + $8M follow-on | Source: [Rice News](https://news.rice.edu/news/2019/feds-fund-creation-headset-high-speed-brain-link) | [Rice Engineering](https://engineering.rice.edu/news-events/brain-brain-communication-demo-receives-darpa-funding)  
Approach: Diffuse optical tomography for reading neural activity (visual cortex) + magnetogenetics for writing. The writing system delivers viral vectors by **ultrasound** to specific neurons, which then express iron-nanoparticle-tethered ion channels that open in response to magnetic heating. Quote from Robinson: *"In four years we hope to demonstrate direct, brain-to-brain communication at the speed of thought and without brain surgery."*

**D. Carnegie Mellon University**  
PI: Pulkit Grover | Award: $19.48M | Source: [CMU News](https://www.cmu.edu/news/stories/archives/2019/may/darpa-brain-interface.html)  
Approach: Acousto-optical neural recording + interfering electrical fields for writing. **AFRL 711HPW personnel (Andy McKinley, Justin Estepp) are listed as collaborators on human demonstration studies** — the clearest documented connection between N3 and AFRL 711HPW.

**E. Battelle Memorial Institute (BrainSTORMS)**  
PI: Gaurav Sharma | Award: ~$20M | Source: [Battelle press release](https://www.battelle.org/insights/newsroom/press-release-details/battelle-led-team-wins-darpa-award-to-develop-injectable-bi-directional-brain-computer-interface)  
Approach: Magnetoelectric nanotransducers injected into bloodstream; no FUS write mechanism; not directly Piezo-relevant.

**F. Johns Hopkins APL**  
PI: David Blodgett | Amount: Not public | Source: [JHU APL news release](https://www.jhuapl.edu/news/news-releases/190624-operating-speed-thought)  
Approach: Coherent optical system for both reading and writing; completely noninvasive; no FUS or Piezo relevance.

**Aggregate mechanosensitive relevance:** Three of six N3 performers (Teledyne, PARC, CMU) use ultrasound as a neural write or read mechanism. By the physics of FUS neuromodulation, these mechanisms implicitly depend on mechanosensitive ion channels at the neuronal membrane — but N3 contracts do not name Piezo channels in available public abstracts. This is an inference from the published science, not a confirmed program-level claim.

Technical reports are Distribution C (limited release) per report number AFRL-RH-FS-TR-2022-2023. The name "TWITCH" is suggestive of a functional neuromodulation capability (involuntary muscle movement via transcranial microwave stimulation), but no unclassified abstract specifically confirms this.

**Key published paper:**  
Echchgadda I, et al. "Changes in the excitability of primary hippocampal neurons following exposure to 3.0 GHz radiofrequency electromagnetic fields." *Scientific Reports.* 2022;12:3506. [PMC8894459](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8894459/)  
Findings: 3.0 GHz RF-EMF at sub-thermal doses (<1 W/kg) depolarizes resting membrane potential and increases intracellular Ca²⁺ in primary rat hippocampal neurons. **Mechanism attributed to voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels — no mention of Piezo channels in the paper.**

**Honesty note:** The TWITCH program's technical reports are restricted. The publicly available ORISE fellowship solicitations describe "identifying neurological effects of RF EMF exposures" and "elucidate the underlying molecular mechanisms of action" but do not mention Piezo channels or mechanosensitive channels. Whether internal AFRL research has investigated Piezo channels as RF-EMF transducers cannot be determined from public records.

### 6. ARPA-H Sonogenetics Award (2026) — Adjacent Agency

**Agency:** Advanced Research Projects Agency for Health (ARPA-H), HHS [not DoD]  
**Award:** D26AC50003-00  
**Date:** February 23, 2026  
**Amount:** Up to $41.3M over ~5.5 years  
**Prime Awardee:** Salk Institute for Biological Studies, San Diego, CA  
**PI:** Sreekanth Chalasani, PhD (Associate Professor, Molecular Neurobiology Laboratory)  
**Collaborating institutions:** Scripps Research, MIT, Duke University; commercial arm: SonoNeu ($5.2M subaward)  
**URL:** [ARPA-H award page](https://arpa-h.gov/explore-funding/awards/3941) | [Salk press release](https://www.salk.edu/news-release/salk-institute-to-lead-arpa-h-project-with-up-to-41-3m-to-advance-sonogenetics-as-a-noninvasive-therapeutic/)

**Scope from ARPA-H award abstract:**  
> "The performer team will develop a method to treat conditions with ultrasound by using gene therapy to express ultrasound-sensitive proteins in specific cells—a concept called sonogenetics. Sonogenetics is a new alternative to pharmaceuticals that can directly change cellular activity with temporal and spatial precision. If successful, the team will develop a new, non-pharmaceutical modality for treating chronic conditions."

**Background:** Chalasani pioneered sonogenetics at Salk beginning in 2011, identifying the TRP-4 mechanosensitive channel in *C. elegans* as the molecule responsible for ultrasound sensitivity. That channel, when ectopically expressed in non-sensitive neurons, conferred ultrasound responsiveness. The 2015 *Nature Communications* paper from his lab demonstrated this. The 2026 ARPA-H award is the **first large federal investment** specifically in therapeutic sonogenetics. Prior Chalasani funding was primarily NIH BRAIN Initiative grants; no prior DARPA BTO funding for Chalasani specifically was confirmed.

**Relationship to Piezo channels:** TRP-4 (the *C. elegans* channel used in original sonogenetics) is a TRPN family channel, not a Piezo channel. However, the program's mechanistic goal — sensitizing specific cells to ultrasound via expressed mechanosensitive proteins — is directly adjacent to Piezo channel biology.

**Defense adjacency:** ARPA-H is an HHS agency modeled on DARPA's program-manager-driven, fixed-duration structure. The peripheral neuropathy focus is relevant to warfighter health (blast injury, diabetic neuropathy in veteran populations). The award states potential applications in brain-computer interfaces. Whether DoD will eventually co-fund or adopt sonogenetics technology is unknown.

### 9. Walter Reed Army Institute of Research (WRAIR) — Microwave Auditory Research

**Historical background:** WRAIR scientists Joseph Sharp and Mark Grove demonstrated in 1975 that amplitude-modulated microwave pulses could transmit intelligible speech sounds via the microwave auditory effect (Frey effect). This remained classified for years.

**Ronald Seaman** (McKesson Bioservices Corp, at WRAIR Medical Research Detachment) published the most detailed post-Cold-War analysis: "Transmission of microwave-induced intracranial sound to the inner ear is most likely through cranial aqueducts" (~2002). [Seaman paper](https://beperk.dobs.com/images/stories/Wetenschap/Tinnitus/Microwave_Hearing_Pathway.pdf)

**Post-2015 WRAIR:** No new public WRAIR publications on the microwave auditory effect or RF-neural manipulation were found. WRAIR's recent focus is TBI, blast injury, PTSD, and infectious disease. The microwave auditory research program appears dormant in the public record.

**Mechanosensitive relevance:** The microwave auditory effect is mediated by thermoelastic expansion in cochlear tissue — a form of mechanoacoustic transduction that could theoretically involve Piezo2 (which is expressed in hair cells). However, no published research linking the microwave auditory effect to Piezo channels was found.

## The Piezo-FUS Research Landscape: DoD vs. Non-DoD

The following table distinguishes DoD-funded from non-DoD-funded key Piezo-FUS papers, to correctly attribute scientific credit:

| Paper | Year | Journal | Piezo Finding | DoD Funding? |
|-------|------|---------|--------------|--------------|
| Hoffman et al. "FUS excites action potentials…in part through PIEZO2" | 2022 | PNAS | PIEZO2 mediates FUS activation of peripheral sensory neurons | **YES — DARPA HR0011-15-2-0054** |
| Zhu et al. "Piezo1 contributes to ultrasound neuromodulation" | 2023 | PNAS | Piezo1 is major mediator of FUS neuromodulation in motor cortex in vivo | No (HK PolyU/Chinese funding) |
| Qiu et al. "Piezo1 Significantly Mediates Ultrasonic Stimulation" | 2019 | iScience | Piezo1 mediates in vitro FUS stimulation | No (HK PolyU) |
| Xu et al. "Piezo1 and Piezo2 Mediate Motor Response to tFUS" | 2024 | (journal) | Both Piezo1 and Piezo2 mediate tFUS-induced motor responses | No (Chinese institutions) |

**Conclusion:** The *PNAS 2022* Hoffman et al. paper is the **only peer-reviewed publication directly linking DoD funding to Piezo channel mediation of FUS neuromodulation**. All other key Piezo-FUS mechanistic papers were funded by Chinese or other non-DoD sources.

## Limitations and Research Gaps

1. **DTIC inaccessibility:** All attempts to retrieve DTIC technical reports from discover.dtic.mil returned access errors. Distribution-controlled reports (e.g., AFRL-RH-FS-TR-2022-2023, the TWITCH technical report) were not retrieved. These reports may contain relevant mechanistic findings.

2. **SBIR.gov search failure:** Direct SBIR.gov API and search URL access failed. It is possible that relevant SBIR/STTR awards exist for DoD components (especially Army, Navy, or Air Force) that name mechanosensitive channels or FUS neuromodulation but were not discovered via secondary searches.

3. **USASpending.gov advanced search not completed:** The federal spending database was not searched directly for "mechanosensitive" or "piezo" as contract description keywords. Small contracts (under $250K) may not surface in secondary searches.

4. **Classified programs:** The existence of classified DoD research on mechanosensitive channel manipulation, sonogenetics, or FUS-based covert neuromodulation cannot be confirmed or denied. The TWITCH program's restricted technical reports are suggestive of sensitivity but not conclusive. The AFRL RF Bioeffects Branch's focus on transcranial neuromodulation via microwave is consistent with national security relevance but disclosed in unclassified terms.

5. **Non-DoD federal research not fully searched:** NIH BRAIN Initiative awards for FUS neuromodulation and Piezo channels are numerous (e.g., R01EB027576 cofunded the Columbia PIEZO2 paper alongside DARPA). A comprehensive NIH RePORTER search for DoD-cofunded FUS/Piezo grants was not completed due to site access issues in this session.

6. **International programs excluded:** The most mechanistically advanced Piezo-FUS research (Piezo1 knockout studies, Piezo2 motor cortex studies) is Chinese-funded. These are not covered in this report's scope but are highly relevant to understanding the global technological landscape.
