# Akwei v. NSA — Web Verification Findings
## Source-Cited Evidence Table

**Research conducted:** Authoritative public sources including court records (PACER/Scribd court documents), The Intercept, ProPublica, New York Times, EFF-hosted NSA ANT catalog PDF, Google Patents (USPTO), Wikipedia (ANT catalog, FAIRVIEW), Schneier on Security, MIT News, PubMed/PMC, and official NSA document archives via journalists.

**Methodology note:** This report distinguishes existence of court documents and technologies from proof of application against any person. Confidence ratings reflect quality of source material and corroboration, not assessment of underlying claims.

## SECTION 4: NSA ANT Catalog — CTX4000 and PHOTOANGLO

| Claim | Source(s) | Finding | Confidence | Caveats |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| The NSA ANT catalog is a real classified document, version 2008–2009, published by Der Spiegel in December 2013 | [Wikipedia: ANT catalog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ANT_catalog); [EFF-hosted PDF of catalog](https://www.eff.org/files/2014/01/06/20131230-appelbaum-nsa_ant_catalog.pdf); [Schneier on Security](https://www.schneier.com/blog/archives/2014/01/ctx4000_nsa_exp.html) | **CONFIRMED.** The ANT catalog was published December 29, 2013, by Der Spiegel (authors: Jacob Appelbaum, Christian Stöcker, Judith Horchert). It is available as a PDF hosted by EFF. The document bears NSA internal classification markings: "TOP SECRET//COMINT//REL TO USA, FVEY." | HIGH | The ANT catalog's leaker is NOT definitively identified as Snowden; Wikipedia and Bruce Schneier note it was "believed to come from a second leaker," not Snowden's cache. NSA did not officially authenticate the document. |
| CTX4000 is listed in the ANT catalog as a portable continuous wave (CW) radar unit for illuminating target systems | [EFF-hosted NSA ANT catalog PDF](https://www.eff.org/files/2014/01/06/20131230-appelbaum-nsa_ant_catalog.pdf); [Schneier on Security](https://www.schneier.com/blog/archives/2014/01/ctx4000_nsa_exp.html); [Wikimedia Commons image](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:NSA_CTX4000.jpg) | **CONFIRMED.** Catalog entry (dated 8 Jul 2008): CTX4000 is described as "a portable continuous wave (CW) radar unit" operating at 1–2 GHz, bandwidth up to 45 MHz, output power adjustable up to 2W (or up to 1kW with external amplifiers), remote controllable. Primary uses: "VAGRANT and DROPMIRE collection." Status: "operational but reaching end of service life; scheduled to be replaced by PHOTOANGLO starting in September 2008." | HIGH | The catalog describes a signals intelligence hardware tool for use against foreign targets with physical implants already placed. It is not described as a remote brain-reading device; it works in conjunction with physical RF retroreflector implants (the ANGRYNEIGHBOR family), which must first be physically installed. |
| PHOTOANGLO is listed as a joint NSA/GCHQ successor to CTX4000 | [EFF-hosted NSA ANT catalog PDF](https://www.eff.org/files/2014/01/06/20131230-appelbaum-nsa_ant_catalog.pdf); [Infosec Institute analysis](https://www.infosecinstitute.com/resources/general-security/nsa-monitors-target-computers-radar-wave-devices/); [Wikipedia: ANT catalog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ANT_catalog) | **CONFIRMED.** Catalog entry (dated 24 Jul 2008): PHOTOANGLO is "a joint NSA/GCHQ project to develop a new radar system to take the place of the CTX4000." Specs: 1–4 GHz frequency range, max bandwidth 450 MHz, fits in "slim briefcase," weight <10 lbs, output 2W. Unit cost: $40,000 (planned). Status: "Development. Planned IOC is 1st QTR FY09." | HIGH | Same caveat as CTX4000: operates in tandem with physically installed retroreflector implants. It is not a standalone remote surveillance tool; requires prior physical access to target hardware to install the corresponding bug. |
| CTX4000/PHOTOANGLO can be used for remote brain monitoring or thought reading | No authoritative source | **NOT ESTABLISHED.** The catalog explicitly describes these as tools for recovering "off net" information from compromised computing hardware (VAGRANT = video signal recovery, DROPMIRE = printer/fax data). There is no mention of biomedical or neural applications in the catalog. The peer-reviewed literature on the microwave auditory (Frey) effect confirms that microwave-induced auditory sensations exist at close range and high power but concludes that "weaponizing" it for covert neural monitoring at a distance remains unproven and likely not feasible at the power levels described. | LOW | The PMC/Frontiers in Public Health peer-reviewed review (2021) found that microwave auditory effects are real phenomena but that "there are easier ways to harass or harm an adversary" and the capability for covert neural reading remains unsubstantiated. |

## SECTION 7: Contextual / Cross-Cutting Assessment

| Issue | Finding | Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| **Critical distinction: 1992 complaint vs. 1996 Nexus dossier** | The original 7-page 1992 court filing is a personal harassment complaint alleging misuse of NSA resources by former acquaintances. The 1996 Nexus dossier is a separate, extended document making sweeping claims about NSA surveillance technology programs applied to Akwei personally. The two are routinely conflated online. | The 1992 complaint is a verifiable court document with known metadata. The Nexus dossier has no independent corroboration and cannot be verified as an authentic court submission — it was not filed with the court. |
| **Scientific plausibility of RNM as described** | The Nexus dossier claims NSA can decode any person's thoughts via remote EMF scanning of the entire U.S. population. Peer-reviewed neuroscience and the biomedical literature (PMC 2021 review of microwave auditory effect) find no evidence supporting remote mass thought-reading at the described capabilities. | The state of peer-reviewed science as of 2024 does not support population-scale remote neural monitoring as described in the dossier. Localized, contact-based neurostimulation (TMS, DBS) and close-range acoustic/microwave effects on perception are real; mass covert remote thought-reading is not established. |
| **The FAIRVIEW/AT&T material is independently real but unrelated to Akwei** | FAIRVIEW, SAGUARO, 4211 Bryan Street are established from 2015–2018 reporting based on Snowden-era documents and former AT&T employees. These involve signals intelligence collection of communications metadata and internet traffic, not neural monitoring. | Citing FAIRVIEW as corroboration for Akwei's RNM claims is a category error. FAIRVIEW is about bulk communications surveillance, not directed energy or brain monitoring. |
| **Patent chain establishes real technologies exist; does not prove weaponization against persons** | Malech (1976), Puharich (1971), Flanagan (1968/1972), Loos (2003), and Norris HSS are all real patents with verifiable USPTO records. Some underlying phenomena (microwave auditory effect, parametric acoustics) have peer-reviewed scientific literature. | Patent existence confirms that researchers have explored EM effects on the nervous system. It does not establish that any of these patents were deployed covertly against the public or against Akwei specifically. The patents describe devices that require either physical contact, close proximity, or implanted hardware — not the claimed continent-scale remote deployment. |
| **The ANT catalog CTX4000/PHOTOANGLO items are real NSA tools but narrowly scoped** | The EFF-hosted ANT catalog PDF is an authentic classified NSA document (2008–2009 version). CTX4000 and PHOTOANGLO are real signals intelligence tools. | They operate by illuminating a previously-installed RF retroreflector implant inside a target computer or cable — not by remotely accessing human brains. The operational requirement for prior physical access to install the implant fundamentally limits the claimed capabilities. |
