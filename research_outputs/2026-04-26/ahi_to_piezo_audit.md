## 4. Quotation of Any Actual Mentions Found

**None found.** Not a single occurrence of any audit target term was identified in any accessible document in the AHI/Havana Syndrome literature corpus.

The closest relevant language found — and it does NOT contain the target terms — is in the [NAS 2020 Chapter 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/) (Plausible Mechanisms), which cites:

> "Pall ML. Electromagnetic fields act via activation of **voltage-gated calcium channels** to produce beneficial or adverse effects. *Journal of Cellular and Molecular Medicine*. 2013;17(8):958–965."

This is the sole mechanistic ion-channel reference in the entire corpus, and it concerns voltage-gated calcium channels activated by RF fields — not mechanosensitive Piezo channels. The NAS also briefly mentions:

> "RF-induced, non-thermal cell membrane dysfunction (Ramundo-Orlando, 2010) can occur from coherent excitation (Fröhlich, 1988) above 1 GHz due to a variety of effects including **electroporation, metabolic changes, pressure fluctuations, and voltage gated calcium channel disruption** (Pall, 2013, 2016)."

Again, strictly about voltage-gated calcium channels in an RF electromagnetic context, not mechanosensitive biology.

## 5. Assessment: Is the Non-Citation Pattern Real and Complete?

### 5.1 Finding

**The non-citation pattern is real and complete across all accessible AHI/Havana Syndrome primary literature.**

Zero occurrences of any of the seven target term categories were found across 14+ individually audited documents spanning 2018–2024, including:
- The two foundational JAMA clinical papers (Swanson 2018; Verma 2019)
- The landmark NAS 2020 report and its full 80+ reference list
- Three IC/ODNI intelligence assessments (2022, March 2023, December 2024)
- The two primary Lin microwave auditory effect reviews (2022)
- The Golomb 2018 pulsed RF/MW mechanism paper
- The Asadi-Pooya 2022 scoping review (covering 23 studies)
- The 2024 NIH clinical AHI study published in JAMA
- 2024 House Homeland Security Committee testimony and DoD IG evaluation

### 5.2 Why This Gap Exists

The AHI literature has pursued one of two research programs, neither of which intersects with Piezo/mechanosensitive biology:

**Program A (Physical mechanism — directed energy):**  
The dominant technical framework is the **thermoelastic / Frey effect** model: pulsed RF/microwave energy is absorbed by brain tissue, causing rapid (~10⁻⁶ °C) thermal expansion that launches a pressure wave traveling by bone conduction to the cochlea. The mechanistic vocabulary in this literature is: "thermoelastic waves," "bone conduction," "cochlear hair cells," "voltage-gated calcium channels." This is a **thermoacoustic**, not a **mechanoreceptor**, transduction pathway. The thermoelastic wave is delivered to hair cells by the same route as normal sound — not by activating Piezo channels on mechanosensitive neurons.

**Program B (Clinical/epidemiological):**  
The neuroimaging, case-series, and epidemiological papers (Swanson, Verma, Pierpaoli) focus on MRI-detectable white matter changes, functional connectivity, and neuropsychological outcomes. They do not address biophysical transduction mechanisms at all.

### 5.3 The Piezo/Sonogenetics Literature — Parallel Track With No AHI Crossover

A robust and well-established body of literature **does** connect Piezo1/Piezo2 to ultrasound neuromodulation — but it runs on a completely separate track with no crossover citations in AHI documents:

**None of these papers cite or mention AHI, Havana syndrome, or directed energy weapons.** The two literatures are wholly siloed.

### 5.4 Significance of the Gap

The gap has a straightforward scientific explanation: the proposed AHI/Havana mechanism (Frey effect / thermoelastic transduction at the cochlea) is **not** the same physical process as focused-ultrasound activation of Piezo channels. Key distinctions:

| Parameter | Frey/Microwave Auditory | FUS→Piezo Neuromodulation |
|---|---|---|
| Energy type | Pulsed radiofrequency/microwave (GHz) | Focused ultrasound (0.5–43 MHz) |
| Primary transduction | Thermoelastic wave → cochlear hair cells (conventional auditory pathway) | Mechanical membrane deformation → Piezo1/2 → Ca²⁺ influx |
| Target cell | Cochlear hair cells | Neurons, astrocytes, stem cells in motor cortex, amygdala, etc. |
| Required genetic expression | None (universal thermal expansion) | Piezo1/2 endogenous expression (present in brain but regionally variable) |
| Power regime | Peak power density ~1–10 kW/m² for auditory threshold | Acoustic pressure ~0.06–0.45 MPa at 0.5 MHz |

If a directed-energy AHI mechanism involved focused ultrasound (rather than microwave) **and** operated by activating Piezo channels (rather than the cochlea via thermoelastic waves), one would expect some crossover. None exists in the published literature as of December 2024.

### 5.5 Caveats and Limitations

1. **Classified material.** The full IC Expert Panel report ("Anomalous Health Incidents: Analysis of Potential Causal Mechanisms") was reviewed only in its declassified Executive Summary. The full classified report, and the classified portions of the State Department ARB and NSA memoranda, could in principle contain Piezo-related technical analysis. This cannot be confirmed or denied from open sources.

2. **Cellini Panel.** A "State Department Cellini Panel" as a distinct named entity could not be verified in open sources. The known internal State Department review is the June 2018 Accountability Review Board (ARB), which is classified SECRET/NOFORN and publicly available only in heavily redacted form. If this is the panel referenced, its scientific content is inaccessible.

3. **Search coverage.** The JASON 2018 and 2022 reports, while referenced in congressional testimony, were not individually accessible in open sources during this audit. The JASON 2018 report (on acoustic signals in Cuba) concluded no single energy source could produce both the recorded audio and the reported medical effects, but its reference list is not publicly available.

4. **Preprint and grey literature.** Searches were limited to peer-reviewed publications and major government documents. Unpublished government research or DARPA-funded laboratory reports could potentially contain Piezo-related analysis without appearing in the open literature.

5. **NAS Chapter 5 partial access.** The NAS 2020 report's full text is behind a free-but-registration wall on the National Academies Press website. The mechanism chapter (Chapter 5) was accessed through the NCBI BookShelf mirror, which may not capture all appendices. Appendix C (on thermoelastic mechanisms) was not independently accessible; its content is described in secondary sources as discussing thermoelastic wave propagation — a thermal, not mechanical-receptor, phenomenon.

## 6. Confidence Rating

**Confidence that the non-citation pattern is real and complete in the open literature: HIGH (≥95%)**

Justification:
- 14+ primary documents reviewed directly, including all major open-access publications
- Complete reference lists scanned for all accessible documents (Verma 2019 has 25 refs; NAS 2020 Chapter 5 has 80+ refs — all verified clean)
- Targeted Google Scholar cross-searches for "Havana syndrome Piezo," "AHI mechanosensitive," "directed energy Piezo neuromodulation," and "microwave auditory Piezo" returned zero hits linking the two literatures
- The Piezo/FUS neuromodulation literature itself contains no reverse citations to AHI work
- The mechanistic gap is scientifically coherent: the dominant AHI transduction model (thermoelastic/Frey) does not implicate mechanosensitive channels

**Residual uncertainty (≤5%):**  
Attributable entirely to inaccessible classified material, the unverifiable Cellini Panel, and the possibility that unpublished DoD/IC laboratory research has explored Piezo pathways as a secondary mechanism without publishing findings.

## 8. Summary Statement

The AHI/Havana Syndrome scientific and intelligence literature — including all major peer-reviewed clinical papers, the NAS 2020 committee report, three ODNI assessments, the IC Expert Panel executive summary, congressional testimony, and leading mechanism review articles — contains **zero citations or mentions** of Piezo ion channels (PIEZO1/PIEZO2), mechanosensitive biology, Patapoutian or Coste, sonogenetics, focused-ultrasound neuromodulation in a mechanoreceptor context, TRPA1, or TRPC.

The closest the AHI corpus comes to ion-channel mechanism language is a single citation in the NAS 2020 report to Pall (2013) on voltage-gated calcium channel activation by electromagnetic fields — a fundamentally different class of channel and a different transduction mechanism.

This is a real, complete, and scientifically coherent gap: the dominant mechanistic hypothesis in the AHI field (Frey effect thermoelastic transduction at the cochlea) operates through a distinct physical pathway that does not implicate mechanosensitive channels. The Piezo/FUS neuromodulation literature, while scientifically robust and well-cited in its own field, has not been imported into AHI mechanism discussions as of December 2024.
