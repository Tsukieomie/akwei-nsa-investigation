# PIEZO1/PIEZO2 Anatomy × AHI Symptom Matrix

**Purpose:** Map PIEZO1 and PIEZO2 tissue expression against the documented Anomalous Health Incident (AHI) / Havana Syndrome symptom clusters, assess mechanistic plausibility, and identify gaps.

**Compiled:** 2025 | **Status:** Research synthesis, not a clinical document

### IC Experts Panel 2022 — Four "Core Characteristics"

> "Four 'core characteristics' were prominent among these AHIs: the acute onset of **audio-vestibular sensory phenomena**, sometimes including sound or pressure in only one ear or on one side of the head; other nearly simultaneous signs and symptoms such as **vertigo, loss of balance, and ear pain**; a **strong sense of locality or directionality**; and the **absence of known environmental or medical conditions** that could have caused the reported signs and symptoms." — [IC Experts Panel 2022](https://www.dni.gov/files/ODNI/documents/assessments/2022_02_01_AHI_Executive_Summary_FINAL_Redacted.pdf)

> "The perception of sound and pain within only one ear suggests the stimulation of its mechanoreceptors, **a specific cranial nerve, or nuclei in the brainstem**, all of which mediate hearing and balance." — ibid.

### Canonical Symptom Clusters (Composite from All Sources)

**Cluster A — Auditory/Acoustic:** Perceived localized sound (buzzing, grinding, humming, squealing), sound in one ear only, ear pain, hearing change, tinnitus. Prevalence: acute sound 86%, ear pain 33% acute ([Swanson 2018](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2673168)).

**Cluster B — Vestibular:** Vertigo, dizziness, loss of balance, nausea. Balance impairment objective in 81%. Peripheral vestibulopathy confirmed in 4/13 by caloric testing.

**Cluster C — Cognitive:** Memory problems (76%), mental fog (76%), impaired concentration (71%), processing speed deficits, executive function impairment. "Good day–bad day" pattern.

**Cluster D — Headache/Pressure:** Headache (76%), intense head pressure (24% acute). Exacerbated by cognitive tasks (62%), oculomotor therapy. New headache type distinct from prior.

**Cluster E — Cutaneous/Somatic Sensory:** Localized facial pressure, skin "pull," localized warmth. Reported anecdotally in State Dept and diplomatic accounts; not systematically quantified in JAMA 2018 but documented in supplemental State Department records and later accounts. [Wikipedia/Havana syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Havana_syndrome): "intense pressure or vibration in the head."

**Cluster F — Visual/Oculomotor:** Light sensitivity (62%), blurred vision, difficulty reading (57%), eye strain. Convergence insufficiency (52%), smooth pursuit dysfunction (52%), saccadic dysfunction (47%) ([Swanson 2018](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2673168)).

**Cluster G — Sleep:** Reduced sleep duration (71%), difficulty falling asleep (67%), daytime fatigue (76%). Required pharmacologic intervention 71%.

**Cluster H — Tinnitus (Persistent):** Tinnitus in 57% at >3 months. Distinct from acute sound phenomena. May represent cochlear or central auditory pathway injury.

## PART 2: PIEZO1 EXPRESSION ATLAS

PIEZO1 (FAM38A; gene ID ENSG00000103335) is a large (~2,521 aa) homotrimeric mechanosensitive cation channel that gates in response to membrane tension/shear stress. It is broadly expressed in non-neuronal tissues, with highest expression in vascular and epithelial cells.

### PIEZO1 Expression by Tissue — Verified Literature

| Tissue / Cell Type | Expression Level | Evidence | Citation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vascular endothelium (systemic) | **High** | Required for embryonic vascular development; shear stress sensor; loss → vascular remodeling defects | [Li et al. PNAS 2014](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1409233111) |
| Cerebrovascular endothelium (BBB) | **High** | Primary mechanosensor in brain capillary ECs; highest Piezo1 expression in CNS by transcriptomics; regulates functional hyperemia | [Nature Comms 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-024-52969-0); [Alzheimer's & Dementia 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12706126/) |
| Brain endothelial cells (BBB integrity) | **High** | Piezo1 ECKO reduces BBB disruption after ischemia; expression upregulated by mechanical stress | [CMLS 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12204977/) |
| Red blood cells (erythrocytes) | **High** | Principal mechanosensor for osmotic regulation; GoF → dehydrated stomatocytosis | [eLife 2015](https://elifesciences.org/articles/07370) |
| Lymphatic endothelium | **Moderate–High** | Required for lymphatic valve development | — |
| Urothelium | **Moderate** | Bladder stretch sensing | — |
| Articular cartilage (chondrocytes) | **Moderate** | Matrix compression sensing | — |
| Meningeal nerves | **Present** | Piezo1 + Piezo2 detected in meningeal sensory nerve fibers; pulsatile activation proposed for migraine | [IJMS 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7037473/) |

| Retinal ganglion cells (RGCs) | **Present** | Piezo1 in soma and axons of RGCs; RPE/choroid highest; agonist Yoda1 increases RGC firing | [IOVS 2024](https://iovs.arvojournals.org/article.aspx?articleid=2799708); [bioRxiv 2024](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2024.06.25.599602v1.full-text) |

| Skeletal muscle | **Present** | Stretch-induced ATP release; lower level | — |

### PIEZO1 — Critical Functional Roles Relevant to AHI

1. **Cerebrovascular blood flow control:** EC Piezo1 acts as a "mechano-feedback brake" on functional hyperemia; GOF suppresses, KO enhances cerebral blood flow increases during neural stimulation. Impairment of this mechanism could alter neurovascular coupling and produce cognitive symptoms. ([Nature Comms 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-024-52969-0))

2. **BBB integrity:** Mechanical activation of Piezo1 in brain ECs disrupts tight junction proteins (ZO-1, Occludin), increasing BBB permeability via Ca²⁺/CaMKII → oxidative stress → inflammation. ([CMLS 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12204977/))

3. **Intracranial pressure sensing:** Piezo1 in neurons upregulated by elevated ICP (in SAH models); neuronal apoptosis via Hippo pathway. ([CNS Neurosci Ther 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11427798/))

4. **Meningeal mechanosensing:** Piezo1 present in meningeal sensory fibers; pulsating dural vessels as the only source of mechanical activation → migraine-like pulsating pain signal. ([IJMS 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7037473/))

## PART 3: PIEZO2 EXPRESSION ATLAS

## PART 4: OTHER MECHANOSENSORS (COMPARISON CHANNELS)

### TMC1/TMC2 — The Primary Cochlear Transduction Channel

TMC1 and TMC2 (Transmembrane Channel-Like proteins) are the pore-forming components of the mechanotransduction channel at stereocilia tip links in cochlear and vestibular hair cells. TMC1 is the adult cochlear channel; TMC2 is transient in cochlea but persists in vestibular hair cells. Loss of TMC1/TMC2 abolishes MET current entirely. ([PubMed TMC1/2 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23871232/); [ScienceDirect review](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0959438825000571))

**Role in AHI:** TMC1 is the primary target for the microwave auditory effect. Pulsed radiofrequency energy causes thermoelastic expansion of brain tissue → pressure wave → travels by bone conduction to cochlea → activates TMC1 in hair cells, producing the Frey/microwave auditory effect (perceived click/buzz). This is a well-established mechanism. ([Microwave Auditory Effect, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave_auditory_effect); [Frey effect papers](https://beperk.dobs.com/images/stories/Wetenschap/Tinnitus/The_Microwave_Auditory_Phenomenon.pdf))

### TRPA1 — Cochlear Damage Sensor / Nociceptor

TRPA1 is expressed in cochlear supporting cells (Hensen's cells) and somatosensory nociceptors. In the cochlea, it acts as an oxidative stress "damage sensor" that initiates a protective mechanism after acoustic trauma — activating supporting cell contraction that temporarily reduces hearing threshold (protective earplug effect). TRPA1 is also present in DRG and trigeminal pain fibers. ([Nature Comms 2023 TRPA1 cochlea](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-023-39589-w))

**Role in AHI:** TRPA1 in nociceptors of the trigeminal ganglia could mediate facial pain/pressure and ear pain. Its role in cochlear damage sensing could explain post-event tinnitus and temporary hearing threshold shifts. However, TRPA1 is a nociceptive/inflammatory channel, not a directional mechanosensor — it cannot explain the directional, unilateral onset.

### Frey Effect / Microwave Thermoelastic Mechanism (Non-Channel)

Pulsed RF/microwave energy (200 MHz – 3 GHz) is absorbed by tissue, causing rapid but minuscule temperature rises (~10⁻⁵ °C per pulse), generating thermoelastic pressure waves that travel by bone conduction to the cochlea, activating TMC1/2-containing hair cells. This produces a perceived click or buzz. It does not require PIEZO channels. The IC Experts Panel 2022 concluded this mechanism "plausibly explains the core characteristics." ([IC Experts Panel 2022](https://www.dni.gov/files/ODNI/documents/assessments/2022_02_01_AHI_Executive_Summary_FINAL_Redacted.pdf); [microwave auditory PDF](https://beperk.dobs.com/images/stories/Wetenschap/Tinnitus/The_Microwave_Auditory_Phenomenon.pdf))

## PART 5: MASTER ANATOMY × SYMPTOM MATRIX

**Fit ratings:**
- **Strong fit:** Channel expressed in relevant tissue; known activation produces matching symptom; mechanistic pathway established
- **Plausible:** Channel expressed in tissue; biologically plausible pathway; not directly demonstrated for this symptom
- **Weak:** Channel expressed but peripheral to symptom; mechanism speculative
- **No fit:** Channel absent from relevant tissue; mechanism cannot apply
- **Indirect:** Channel not directly causing symptom but may modulate downstream effects

### CLUSTER A: Auditory Phenomena (Acute perceived sound, click, buzz in one ear)

| Mechanism | Fit | Evidence / Notes |
|-----------|-----|-----------------|
| **PIEZO2 (vestibular/cochlear hair cells)** | **Weak** | PIEZO2 present in OHC cuticular plate; NOT in stereocilia where primary transduction occurs; not responsible for normal sound perception. Unilateral activation by a spatially focused stimulus is biologically possible if tissue deformation occurs asymmetrically, but PIEZO2's role at the cuticular plate is not sound transduction in the conventional sense. The reverse-polarity current it mediates is normally suppressed by Ca²⁺ after development. |
| **PIEZO1 (inner ear)** | **No fit** | Not detected in cochlear or vestibular hair cells by ISH or immunostaining. [Wu et al. 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5191906/) |
| **TMC1/2 (cochlear hair cells)** | **Strong fit** | Primary MET channel; activated by thermoelastic pressure waves (Frey effect) from pulsed RF. Explains the perceived directional sound, unilateral onset, and click/buzz quality. This is the established mechanism. |
| **TRPA1 (cochlea support cells)** | **Weak** | TRPA1 present in Hensen's/supporting cells; activated by oxidative stress after trauma. Cannot explain acute directional sound perception; secondary damage sensor. |
| **Thermal/RF direct (non-PIEZO)** | **Strong fit** | Thermoelastic mechanism → bone-conducted pressure → TMC1 activation. IC Panel 2022 finding. Directional (focal beam) explains unilateral presentation. |

**Honest assessment:** The acute directional auditory phenomena are **best explained by the Frey/thermoelastic mechanism acting on TMC1/2**, not by PIEZO channels. PIEZO2 in OHCs is present but not the primary sound transducer and does not explain directional, unilateral perceived sound. PIEZO1 is absent from the inner ear. **PIEZO does not explain this cluster.**

### CLUSTER B: Vestibular (Vertigo, dizziness, balance loss, oscillopsia)

| Mechanism | Fit | Evidence / Notes |
|-----------|-----|-----------------|
| **PIEZO2 (vestibular hair cells)** | **Plausible** | Confirmed expressed in vestibular hair cells (utricle, saccule, canal cristae) by reporter mice [Wu et al. 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5191906/). Functional role: the J Physiol 2017 review notes PIEZO2 mediates anomalous currents in vestibular hair cells during development and after damage, suggesting a role in sensing epithelial distortion from non-stereociliary forces. Direct activation by focused pressure/acoustic stimulation is plausible. PIEZO2 KO in vestibular system: mild or no defect noted in Wu et al. for low-frequency vestibular responses — suggesting it is not the primary transducer but may modulate sensitivity. |
| **PIEZO2 (proprioceptors — secondary)** | **Weak** | Loss of PIEZO2 proprioception → severe ataxia; conversely, aberrant PIEZO2 activation in proprioceptors could produce mismatch signals interpreted as vestibular disturbance. Speculative for AHI context. |
| **PIEZO1 (vestibular endothelium)** | **Indirect** | Piezo1 in blood vessels of vestibular tissue. Vascular dysfunction → endolymph pressure changes → secondary vestibular effects. Speculative. |
| **TMC1/2 (vestibular hair cells)** | **Plausible** | TMC2 is the primary transduction channel in vestibular hair cells and persists into adulthood. Thermoelastic pressure waves from pulsed RF could directly activate vestibular hair cells via TMC2 if the wave reaches the vestibular epithelium. This plausibly explains acute vertigo. |
| **Thermoelastic / direct pressure (non-PIEZO)** | **Strong fit** | Focused pressure or acoustic energy directly deforming the vestibular epithelium or endolymph. IC Panel 2022: "the perception of sound and pain within only one ear suggests the stimulation of its mechanoreceptors." Explains unilateral vestibular dysfunction. |

**Honest assessment:** PIEZO2 in vestibular hair cells offers a **plausible** contribution — it is present, can be activated by non-bundle forces, and could contribute to abnormal vestibular signaling. However, TMC1/2 (especially TMC2 in vestibular cells) and direct mechanical stimulation of the vestibular epithelium remain stronger explanations for the acute event. PIEZO2 may play a more significant role in **post-event persistent vestibulopathy** if initial injury disrupts the epithelial architecture and unmasks PIEZO2-mediated currents. **Partial PIEZO2 fit (plausible, not strong); primary mechanism likely TMC2/direct pressure.**

### CLUSTER C: Cognitive (Memory problems, "brain fog," concentration deficits, slowed processing)

| Mechanism | Fit | Evidence / Notes |
|-----------|-----|-----------------|
| **PIEZO1 (cerebrovascular endothelium)** | **Plausible–Strong** | PIEZO1 is the primary mechanosensor in brain capillary ECs. Aberrant activation disrupts functional hyperemia (neurovascular coupling), reduces CBF increases during neural activity, and disrupts BBB integrity. Consequences: reduced oxygen/glucose delivery to active neurons → cognitive symptoms. Evidence: GOF Piezo1 in ECs impairs cognitive performance in behavioral tasks [Nature Comms 2024](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-024-52969-0); Piezo1 inhibition protects against cognitive impairment in chronic cerebral hypoperfusion [PubMed 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38401846/). |
| **PIEZO1 (BBB disruption)** | **Plausible** | Piezo1 activation → Ca²⁺/CaMKII → oxidative stress → tight junction protein degradation → BBB permeability → neuroinflammation → cognitive impairment. [CMLS 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12204977/) |
| **PIEZO2 (brain neurons — select)** | **Weak** | Piezo2 expressed in select pyramidal neurons (CA3, neocortex) and Purkinje cells; proposed to sense ICP pulsations; abnormal activation by elevated ICP or blast could alter network synchrony. No direct human cognitive data from Piezo2 activation in brain neurons. [bioRxiv 2020](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2020.03.24.006452.full) |
| **Non-PIEZO (downstream neural injury)** | **Plausible** | Cognitive deficits may reflect secondary neuroinflammation, glial activation, or white matter microstructural changes rather than direct PIEZO activation. NIH 2024 found no MRI-detectable brain injury; re-analysis found subtle salience network connectivity reduction [IJIRMS 2026](https://ijirms.in/index.php/ijirms/article/view/2152). |

**Honest assessment:** **PIEZO1 in cerebrovascular endothelium offers the strongest mechanistic link to cognitive symptoms** — disrupted neurovascular coupling and BBB integrity are well-established downstream consequences of aberrant Piezo1 activation, and these mechanisms match the "brain fog" phenotype. The chronic, low-level nature of cognitive symptoms is consistent with persistent BBB permeability or impaired CBF regulation rather than acute PIEZO activation. **Fit: Plausible to Strong for PIEZO1 via cerebrovascular route.**

### CLUSTER D: Headache and Head Pressure

| Mechanism | Fit | Evidence / Notes |
|-----------|-----|-----------------|
| **PIEZO1 (meningeal sensory nerves)** | **Plausible** | Both Piezo1 and Piezo2 are present in meningeal nociceptive fibers [IJMS 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7037473/). Pulsating dilated dural vessels are the normal source of meningeal mechanosensitive channel activation. Aberrant activation by externally induced pressure waves → trigeminovascular nociceptive signaling → throbbing headache. |
| **PIEZO2 (meningeal nerves)** | **Plausible** | Also present in meningeal fibers. "Piezo channels, specifically Piezo1 and Piezo2, play significant roles in various pain mechanisms. In migraines, these channels are involved in mechanical hypersensitivity." [IJMS 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7037473/) |
| **PIEZO2 (trigeminal ganglia)** | **Plausible** | PIEZO2 present in 20–50% of human TG neurons. Sensitized TG neurons could amplify pain signals from meningeal nociceptors. Mechanical allodynia via PIEZO2 after initial injury: if the dura is sensitized, subsequent PIEZO2-mediated mechanotransduction produces exaggerated pain responses ([Sci Transl Med 2018](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/scitranslmed.aat9892)). |
| **PIEZO1 (cerebrovascular / ICP)** | **Plausible** | Acute pressure wave → transient ICP elevation → Piezo1 activation in neurons (ICH data) → neuronal stress signaling. Head pressure sensation could result from this. |
| **Thermoelastic pressure (non-PIEZO)** | **Plausible** | Direct pressure wave from RF thermoelastic effect could activate meningeal mechanoreceptors acutely, independent of PIEZO. |

**Honest assessment:** Headache and head pressure are the symptom cluster with the **most plausible PIEZO involvement** at multiple levels — meningeal Piezo1/2 for acute pressure sensation, trigeminal Piezo2 for persistent mechanical allodynia, and cerebrovascular Piezo1 for chronic headache via neurovascular dysregulation. This is mechanistically coherent. **Fit: Plausible for both PIEZO1 and PIEZO2.**

### CLUSTER E: Cutaneous / Somatic Sensory (Facial pressure, skin "pull," localized warmth)

| Mechanism | Fit | Evidence / Notes |
|-----------|-----|-----------------|
| **PIEZO2 (trigeminal cutaneous mechanoreceptors)** | **Strong** | PIEZO2 is the primary mechanotransducer in facial skin: expressed in 20–50% of TG neurons, Merkel cells of whisker follicles (face), Meissner corpuscles in facial skin, and in TG nerve terminals. It mediates light-touch, pressure, and sustained deformation detection. Facial "pressure" and skin "pull" sensations are precisely what PIEZO2-expressing mechanoreceptors encode. Direct activation by focal mechanical force or acoustic radiation pressure on facial skin would activate PIEZO2 in facial LTMRs. [Anderson et al. 2017](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5630267/); [Schneider et al. 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6420409/) |
| **PIEZO1 (skin)** | **Weak** | PIEZO1 expressed in skin tissue but not in primary sensory neurons. May be in keratinocytes or vascular cells of skin; no established role in cutaneous mechanosensation. |
| **TRPA1 (nociceptors)** | **Plausible** | TRPA1 in trigeminal C-fibers mediates thermal pain and burning sensations. "Localized warmth" could reflect TRPA1 activation if tissue temperature is locally elevated by RF energy absorption. |
| **RF thermal effect (non-PIEZO)** | **Plausible** | Localized warming from RF absorption in skin/subcutaneous tissue, directly activating TRPV1/TRPA1 thermoreceptors. Not PIEZO-dependent. |

**Honest assessment:** **PIEZO2 offers the strongest mechanistic fit for the mechanical components of Cluster E** — facial pressure and "skin pull" are tactile mechanosensory experiences mediated by PIEZO2-expressing trigeminal afferents and facial Merkel cells. This is one of the best-fit PIEZO2 links in the entire matrix. The warmth component is better explained by TRPA1/TRPV1 or direct RF thermal effects. **PIEZO2 fit: Strong (for pressure/pull); No PIEZO fit for warmth.**

### CLUSTER F: Visual / Oculomotor (Blurred vision, light sensitivity, convergence insufficiency)

| Mechanism | Fit | Evidence / Notes |
|-----------|-----|-----------------|
| **PIEZO1 (retinal ganglion cells)** | **Plausible** | Piezo1 and Piezo2 both expressed in RGC soma and axons. Piezo1 activation (Yoda1) increases RGC firing frequency — abnormal activation could produce photopsia or heightened light sensitivity (photophobia). Stretch-induced upregulation of PIEZO1/2 after single IOP elevation [IOVS 2024](https://iovs.arvojournals.org/article.aspx?articleid=2799708). |
| **PIEZO2 (optic nerve)** | **Plausible** | Highest Piezo2 expression in optic nerve among posterior eye compartments. Optic nerve axons are PIEZO2-positive. Mechanical injury to optic nerve from pressure wave could alter RGC output. |
| **PIEZO1 (cerebrovascular — visual cortex)** | **Indirect** | If Piezo1-mediated BBB disruption or neurovascular uncoupling affects visual cortex perfusion → visual processing deficits. Speculative. |
| **Non-PIEZO (oculomotor nuclei)** | **Plausible** | Convergence insufficiency and smooth pursuit dysfunction in AHI are central signs (brainstem/cerebellum). No PIEZO expression data in oculomotor nuclei. These are more consistent with diffuse white matter injury or central vestibular network disruption. |

**Honest assessment:** **PIEZO1/2 in retinal ganglion cells offers a plausible mechanism for photophobia and primary visual disturbance**, but the oculomotor signs (convergence insufficiency, saccadic dysfunction) are central and likely reflect injury to brainstem oculomotor networks where PIEZO expression is not established. **Fit: Plausible for photophobia/vision via RGC PIEZO1; No PIEZO fit for oculomotor signs.**

### CLUSTER G: Sleep Disturbance (Reduced duration, insomnia, fatigue)

| Mechanism | Fit | Evidence / Notes |
|-----------|-----|-----------------|
| **PIEZO1 (basal forebrain)** | **Plausible** | Sleep deprivation upregulates Piezo1 in basal forebrain, associated with fear memory impairment via Ca²⁺/calpain signaling [ScienceDirect 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0166432821004824). Whether Piezo1 activation *causes* or *responds to* sleep disruption is unclear — current data suggest it's a marker of sleep-deprived state, not necessarily the driver. |
| **PIEZO2 (gut–brain, enterochromaffin cells)** | **Weak** | PIEZO2-initiated gut–brain axis may synchronize with REM sleep via serotonergic EC cell signaling [IJMS 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12346783/). Highly speculative for AHI context. |
| **Non-PIEZO (secondary)** | **Plausible** | Sleep disturbance most likely secondary to headache, anxiety, PTSD, cognitive hyperactivation, and autonomic dysregulation rather than direct PIEZO-mediated sleep pathway disruption. NIH 2024 confirmed significantly elevated PTSD/depression scores in AHI cohort [JAMA 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10949151/). |

**Honest assessment:** **No direct PIEZO mechanism established for sleep disruption.** The evidence for Piezo1 in basal forebrain is correlational (sleep deprivation → Piezo1 upregulation), not causal (Piezo1 activation → sleep disruption). AHI sleep disturbance is most parsimoniously explained by secondary effects: pain, PTSD, hyperarousal. **PIEZO does not explain this cluster at a primary mechanistic level.**

### CLUSTER H: Tinnitus (Persistent auditory symptoms)

| Mechanism | Fit | Evidence / Notes |
|-----------|-----|-----------------|
| **PIEZO2 (OHC — chronic after damage)** | **Plausible** | After acoustic or pressure trauma, tip-link damage could unmask PIEZO2-mediated reverse-polarity currents in OHCs (PIEZO2 suppressed by normal Ca²⁺ levels; revealed when Ca²⁺ drops after tip-link disruption) [Wu et al. 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5191906/). Aberrant PIEZO2 currents in damaged OHCs could contribute to spontaneous firing and tinnitus generation. This is speculative but mechanistically coherent. |
| **TRPA1 (cochlear support cells — post-trauma)** | **Plausible** | After noise/pressure trauma, TRPA1 in cochlear Hensen's cells is activated by lipid peroxidation products → prolonged supporting cell contraction → altered OHC mechanical environment → persistent hearing threshold change and possibly tinnitus. [Nature Comms 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-023-39589-w) |
| **PIEZO1 (inner ear)** | **No fit** | Absent from inner ear. |
| **TMC1 (sensorineural hearing loss)** | **Plausible** | If the thermoelastic pressure wave caused barotrauma to stereocilia → TMC1 disruption → sensorineural hearing loss → central tinnitus adaptation. |
| **Non-PIEZO central mechanisms** | **Likely primary** | Tinnitus in AHI is probably a consequence of initial cochlear injury (whatever the mechanism) followed by central auditory plasticity generating the percept. Not a direct PIEZO activation phenomenon. |

**Honest assessment:** **PIEZO2 in damaged OHCs may contribute to tinnitus through aberrant channel unmasking after initial injury**, but this is a secondary effect rather than the initiating event. The initial cochlear trauma is better explained by thermoelastic/TMC1 mechanisms. **Fit: Weak/Plausible for PIEZO2 (secondary); primary mechanism is non-PIEZO.**

## PART 7: HONEST ASSESSMENT OF FIT

### Where PIEZO channels fit well

**PIEZO1:**
1. **Cognitive symptoms via cerebrovascular dysfunction** — This is the strongest and most mechanistically grounded PIEZO1 link to AHI. Piezo1 is the dominant mechanosensor in brain capillary endothelium; aberrant activation from mechanical waves or pressure disrupts neurovascular coupling and BBB integrity, both of which produce cognitive deficits. The evidence base includes multiple independent studies of Piezo1 GOF and KO models with cognitive behavioral endpoints.
2. **Headache via meningeal mechanosensation** — Piezo1 in dural/meningeal sensory fibers is plausibly activated by pulsed pressure waves, contributing to acute and subacute headache. Supporting data comes from migraine mechanistic research rather than AHI studies specifically.

**PIEZO2:**
1. **Cutaneous facial sensory symptoms** — This is the strongest PIEZO2 link. PIEZO2 is the primary mechanotransducer in trigeminal-innervated facial skin (LTMRs, Merkel cells in whisker/face region). Focal pressure or acoustic radiation force on facial tissue would directly activate PIEZO2, producing the reported "skin pull" and facial pressure sensations. This is mechanistically robust.
2. **Headache/mechanical allodynia** — PIEZO2 in meningeal fibers and TG neurons mediates mechanical allodynia. After an initiating injury, PIEZO2 upregulation or sensitization in TG neurons could amplify pain from normal stimuli, explaining persistent headaches exacerbated by ordinary sensory input.
3. **Vestibular contribution (possible)** — PIEZO2 expression in vestibular hair cells is confirmed; its precise functional role there is less clear than in other tissues. A plausible contribution to post-event vestibular dysfunction exists but lacks direct experimental support in this context.

### Where PIEZO channels do NOT explain AHI symptoms

**Critical gap — Acute auditory phenomena (Cluster A):** The defining feature of AHI — the acute perception of a directional, localized sound in one ear — is NOT explained by PIEZO channel activation. PIEZO1 is absent from the inner ear; PIEZO2 is present in OHC cell bodies but is not the primary sound transduction channel. The directional acoustic percept is best explained by the thermoelastic/Frey effect acting on TMC1/2.

**Sleep disturbance (Cluster G):** No primary PIEZO mechanism. Sleep pathology is secondary to pain, PTSD, and autonomic dysregulation. Available PIEZO/sleep data shows Piezo1 upregulation as a *consequence* of sleep deprivation, not as a driver.

**Tinnitus (Cluster H) — initiation:** While PIEZO2 could contribute secondarily to tinnitus after OHC damage, it is not the initiating mechanism. Initial cochlear injury via TMC1/2-mediated barotrauma or thermoelastic pressure is more parsimonious.

**Oculomotor signs (Cluster F — convergence insufficiency, saccadic dysfunction):** These signs reflect central brainstem/cerebellar pathway disruption. No PIEZO expression data exists for oculomotor nuclei (CN3/4/6, superior colliculus, cerebellum lobules VII/X). Cannot be attributed to PIEZO activation.

**Central vestibular dysfunction (Cluster B — partial):** The central vestibular component (brain network disruption as evidenced by VOR impairment and complex visual/vestibular interaction) is not explained by PIEZO, which would act peripherally (in hair cells or proprioceptors). Central dysfunction is more consistent with white matter tract injury or brainstem network disruption.

## PART 8: MECHANISTIC SYNTHESIS

### Proposed Multi-Stage Model

The AHI symptom profile can be parsed into at least three temporal stages, each with different dominant mechanisms:

**Stage 1 — Acute (seconds to minutes):**
- Pulsed RF/microwave → thermoelastic pressure → skull-conducted acoustic wave → TMC1/2 activation in cochlear and vestibular hair cells → perceived directional sound, vertigo, ear pain
- Simultaneously: direct pressure/acoustic radiation force on facial skin → PIEZO2 activation in TG facial afferents → facial pressure/pull sensation
- Meningeal mechanoreceptors (Piezo1/2) + trigeminal activation → acute headache

**Stage 2 — Subacute (hours to days):**
- TMC1/2-mediated cochlear hair cell trauma → hair bundle disruption → PIEZO2 unmasking in OHCs (tip-link breakage removes Ca²⁺ suppression) → aberrant OHC currents → tinnitus
- Initial BBB disruption from mechanical force on endothelial PIEZO1 → neuroinflammation onset → early cognitive fog
- TRPA1 in cochlear supporting cells activated by oxidative stress products → prolonged threshold shift

**Stage 3 — Chronic (weeks to years):**
- Persistent PIEZO1-mediated cerebrovascular dysregulation → impaired neurovascular coupling → cognitive symptoms
- PIEZO2 sensitization/upregulation in TG neurons → mechanical allodynia → persistent headache exacerbated by normal stimuli
- Central vestibular network reorganization → persistent balance impairment
- PTSD/hyperarousal → sleep disturbance (non-PIEZO)

### Summary of PIEZO Contribution

| Stage | PIEZO1 Role | PIEZO2 Role | Other |
|-------|-------------|-------------|-------|
| Acute | Meningeal headache (minor) | Facial sensory (pressure, pull) | **TMC1/2 (primary)**, Frey effect |
| Subacute | BBB disruption begins | OHC unmasking → tinnitus | TRPA1 cochlea |
| Chronic | **Neurovascular coupling impairment, cognitive symptoms** | Trigeminal allodynia, persistent headache | PTSD, autonomic dysregulation |

## PART 9: KEY EVIDENCE GAPS

1. **No human data on PIEZO1/2 expression in vestibular ganglion** (Scarpa's ganglion). Mouse data confirms vestibular hair cell expression; human vestibular ganglion neuron expression is not established from published literature reviewed here.

2. **No direct evidence that pulsed RF activates PIEZO channels.** Current mechanistic models focus on thermoelastic pressure activating TMC1/2. Whether the same pressure waves are of sufficient amplitude and frequency characteristics to gate Piezo channels (which require distinct membrane tension) is not established experimentally.

3. **PIEZO2 in human trigeminal ganglia** expression level data is limited. Cross-species data (20–50% of mouse TG neurons) is used as a proxy; direct human TG PIEZO2 quantification is needed.

4. **PIEZO2 functional role in vestibular hair cells** is unclear at the specific level. The Wu et al. 2016 paper shows expression and anomalous currents but no vestibular behavioral deficit in Piezo2 KO mice, suggesting the channel is not the primary vestibular transducer.

5. **PIEZO1/2 response to focused RF energy** has not been studied. The plausibility of PIEZO-mediated effects in AHI depends on whether RF-induced thermoelastic pressure waves are of sufficient mechanical nature to activate Piezo channels in relevant tissues.

6. **Sleep and autonomic PIEZO pathways** remain poorly characterized. The gut-brain PIEZO2-serotonin axis ([IJMS 2025](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12346783/)) is highly speculative for AHI.
