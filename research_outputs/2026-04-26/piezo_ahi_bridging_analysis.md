# Piezo / AHI Bridging Analysis

*Investigation into whether the open literature on Piezo ion channels and focused-ultrasound neuromodulation can mechanistically explain Anomalous Health Incident (AHI / "Havana Syndrome") symptoms beyond the cochlear Frey effect.*

**Date:** 2026-04-26
**Author:** pocket-lab
**Status:** Open-source synthesis. Energetics finding is decisive and reframes the working hypothesis.

---

## TL;DR

Going in, the working hypothesis was: *RF → thermoelastic acoustic pulse → PIEZO2 in non-cochlear afferents → non-cochlear AHI symptoms.* The investigation produced four findings that together force a substantial revision of that hypothesis.

1. **The non-citation pattern is real and complete in both directions.** No AHI document cites any Piezo / sonogenetics / mechanosensitive channel work. No Piezo / FUS-neuromodulation paper cites Havana, AHI, Frey, or directed-energy bioeffects. The two literatures are structurally unaware of each other.
2. **The energetics do not support the bridging hypothesis at Frey-class power levels.** Peak acoustic pressure from a Frey-class RF pulse is ~0.02–0.7 Pa in head tissue. The minimum bulk pressure that activates Piezo1 in neurons in vivo is ~20 kPa. The gap is roughly **10⁴ to 10⁶**. It narrows to ~50× only at extreme, non-auditory, weaponizable RF power densities.
3. **The anatomy fits the symptom profile well — for the parts it fits.** PIEZO2 is the right transducer for cutaneous facial pressure / "skin pull" symptoms, plausible for headache and some vestibular symptoms. PIEZO1 is plausible for cognitive symptoms via cerebrovascular / BBB disruption. Acute directional auditory phenomena (the defining symptom) remain best explained by the Frey effect → cochlear hair cells via TMC1/2, not Piezo.
4. **The bridging hypothesis as originally framed (RF→thermoelastic→Piezo) is energetically unsupported.** A revised hypothesis (direct acoustic / FUS-class exposure → Piezo) is energetically plausible but requires a different threat model than RF directed energy.

The bridging paper that the open literature is missing turns out to be a **negative finding paper**, not the positive synthesis I initially described. That's still a publishable and meaningful result — possibly more so.

## 1. The Non-Citation Pattern Is Real

### 1.1 AHI corpus → Piezo / sonogenetics

Across 14+ documents in the AHI literature from 2018 through December 2024 — including the [NAS 2020 report](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/), [JAMA 2018 Swanson](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2673168), [JAMA 2019 Verma](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6652163/), the [IC Expert Panel 2022 executive summary](https://www.dni.gov/files/ODNI/documents/assessments/2022_02_01_AHI_Executive_Summary_FINAL_Redacted.pdf), the [2023](https://www.dni.gov/files/ODNI/documents/assessments/Updated-Assessment-of-Anomalous-Health-Incidents-Unclassified-March-2023.pdf) and [December 2024 ODNI](https://www.dni.gov/files/ODNI/documents/assessments/NIC-Unclassified-ICA-Updated-Assessment-AHI-December2024.pdf) assessments, two [Lin 2022](https://www.academia.edu/83292239/Microwave_Auditory_Effects_Among_U_S_Government_Personnel_Reporting_Directional_Audible_and_Sensory_Phenomena_in_Havana) microwave-auditory reviews, [Golomb 2018](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30183509/), [NIH/Pierpaoli 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10949155/), and 2024 congressional testimony — **zero hits** for any of: Piezo, PIEZO1, PIEZO2, mechanosensitive, mechanotransduction, Patapoutian, Coste, sonogenetic, focused ultrasound (in neuromodulation context), TRPA1, TRPC.

The NAS 2020 report's 80+ reference list was scanned in full. No mechanotransduction citations.

### 1.2 FUS-Piezo corpus → AHI / Frey / directed energy

15 papers and programs audited across the FUS-Piezo and sonogenetics literature: [Hoffman et al. 2022 PNAS](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2115821119), [Coste et al. 2010 Science](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1193270), [Coste et al. 2012 Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature10812), [Prieto et al. 2018](https://web.stanford.edu/~kfirouzi/papers/journals/Prietoetal2017UMB.pdf), [Oh et al. 2019 Current Biology](https://www.cell.com/current-biology/fulltext/S0960-9822%2819%2931390-7), [Yoo et al. 2022 Nat Commun](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8789820/), [Zhu et al. 2023 PNAS](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10161134/), Ibsen/Chalasani sonogenetics work, DARPA N3 publications, UC Berkeley Neural Dust papers, recent reviews. Zero hits across every search term: Havana, AHI, anomalous health, Frey, microwave auditory, directed energy, thermoelastic (RF context), Cuba, weapon.

One important disambiguation: Hoffman 2022 cites "**William Fry**" (acoustic physicist, 1950s CNS ultrasound work). This is a different person from **Allan H. Frey** (microwave biologist, 1961). Not a near-miss.

### 1.3 The closest bridge document

The single document that sits closest to the bridge is [Foster, Garrett & Ziskin 2021, "Can the Microwave Auditory Effect Be Weaponized?"](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8733248/) in *Frontiers in Public Health*. It cites two general FUS neuromodulation reviews for acoustic-pressure context but cites **zero** Piezo / mechanotransduction / sonogenetics papers. No FUS-Piezo paper cites it back. It is the closest neighbor and the gap is still complete.

### 1.4 Researcher public statements

Konofagou (Columbia, the PI on the DARPA-funded PIEZO2/FUS work), Patapoutian, Coste, Chalasani, Ibsen, Maharbiz, Carmena, and all DARPA N3 PIs have made no traceable public statement connecting their work to AHI. Lin JC (the microwave bioeffects researcher who does write about Havana Syndrome) does not cite Piezo or sonogenetics in his AHI papers.

**Confidence on the non-citation finding: ≥95%** in the open literature. Residual uncertainty is in classified material (full IC Expert Panel report, State Department ARB).

## 2. The Energetics Are the Decisive Finding

This is where the hypothesis breaks — or rather, where it has to be rebuilt around a different threat model.

### 2.1 RF thermoelastic pressures (Frey-class)

From [Lin 1977](https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1029/RS012i06Sp00237) and [Lin 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17495664/):

| Condition | Peak Pressure |
|---|---|
| MAE cochlear threshold | **20 mPa = 0.020 Pa** |
| Human adult head, 1 kW/kg SAR, 10 μs | **0.682 Pa** |
| Foster 2021 physical upper limit, stress confinement | **~300–500 Pa** |
| [Dagro 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8555891/) at 10⁶ mW/cm² (non-auditory, weaponizable) | **tens of kPa** |

Temperature rise at the auditory threshold is ~1 μK. The relation is roughly p ≈ 1 Pa per μK of ΔT.

### 2.2 Piezo gating thresholds

From the primary literature ([Coste 2010/2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3508907/), [Lewis & Grandl 2015](https://elifesciences.org/articles/12088), [Cox et al. 2016](https://www.nature.com/articles/ncomms10366), [Hoffman 2022](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2115821119), [Zhu 2023](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10161134/)):

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Piezo1 membrane tension T₅₀ | 1.4–5.1 mN/m |
| Piezo1 minimum bulk FUS pressure (in vivo, neurons) | **~20 kPa** |
| Piezo1 standard FUS neuromodulation pressure | 350–450 kPa |
| Piezo2 DRG via FUS (Hoffman 2022) | est. ~1–3 MPa |

### 2.3 The gap

| Comparison | Ratio |
|---|---|
| RF cochlear threshold (0.02 Pa) vs. minimum FUS-Piezo1 (20 kPa) | **~10⁶×** |
| RF tissue peak at MAE threshold (~0.24 Pa) vs. minimum FUS-Piezo1 | **~84,000×** |
| RF physical upper limit (~400 Pa) vs. minimum FUS-Piezo1 | **~50×** |

Even at the most favorable comparison — the absolute physical upper bound for RF thermoelastic pressure against the most sensitive FUS-Piezo1 detection — the gap is ~50×. And the temporal waveforms don't match: a Frey pulse is a microsecond broadband transient, while FUS-Piezo activation is driven by sustained sinusoidal radiation force / streaming over hundreds of milliseconds. Piezo1's inactivation time constant is on the order of tens of milliseconds; it integrates poorly over a single microsecond transient.

### 2.4 Why this is decisive

There is no known cellular amplification mechanism that multiplies bulk acoustic pressure by 10⁴–10⁶ at the neuronal membrane. The hydrostatic-to-tension coupling for a 10 μm cell under 20 kPa bulk pressure produces only ~0.05 mN/m of membrane tension, already ~50× below Piezo1's T₅₀. At RF auditory threshold pressures, induced membrane tension would be ~10⁻⁷ mN/m — utterly negligible.

**The implication:** the working hypothesis "RF → thermoelastic → Piezo" cannot be defended on physics. RF thermoelastic pressure can plausibly drive cochlear hair cells via bone conduction (the Frey effect, well established), but it cannot plausibly drive Piezo1/2 in extracochlear tissue at any RF power level that doesn't also produce tissue injury.

## 3. The Anatomy Fits — Where It Fits

If the threat is acoustic/FUS-class rather than RF-class (see Section 4), the anatomy lines up cleanly with several but not all AHI symptom clusters.

### 3.1 PIEZO2 — strong / plausible fits

- **Cutaneous facial pressure, "skin pull"** — strong fit. PIEZO2 is the primary mechanotransducer in trigeminally innervated facial skin (Merkel cells, low-threshold mechanoreceptors, Meissner corpuscles). Direct focal acoustic radiation pressure on face/scalp would produce these sensations.
- **Headache (persistent, allodynic)** — plausible. PIEZO2 in meningeal nerve fibers and trigeminal ganglia mediates mechanical allodynia; sensitization could amplify innocuous stimuli into pain.
- **Vestibular symptoms** — plausible. PIEZO2 expression in vestibular hair cells is confirmed; functional role secondary to TMC channels but aberrant currents in damaged epithelium could sustain post-event vestibulopathy.

### 3.2 PIEZO1 — plausible fits

- **Cognitive symptoms (brain fog, memory, concentration)** — plausible to strong. PIEZO1 is the primary mechanosensor in brain capillary endothelium; aberrant activation disrupts neurovascular coupling and BBB integrity. Mouse studies link EC-Piezo1 GOF to impaired cognition.
- **Acute headache** — plausible. PIEZO1 in meningeal sensory fibers; pulsed pressure waves could activate trigeminovascular nociception.

### 3.3 What PIEZO does NOT explain

- **Acute directional auditory phenomena (the defining AHI symptom)** — best explained by Frey effect → bone conduction → cochlear hair cells via TMC1/2. PIEZO1 absent from inner ear; PIEZO2 present at OHC cuticular plate but not the primary sound transduction channel.
- **Sleep disturbance** — no primary PIEZO pathway; secondary to pain / autonomic / PTSD.
- **Oculomotor signs** (convergence insufficiency, saccadic dysfunction) — central brainstem/cerebellar; no PIEZO data in relevant nuclei.
- **Tinnitus initiation** — primary initiation non-PIEZO; PIEZO2 OHC unmasking is a plausible secondary contributor only.

### 3.4 The composite picture

| Symptom cluster | Best mechanism | PIEZO involvement |
|---|---|---|
| Acute directional sound/click/buzz | Frey effect → TMC1/2 | None primary |
| Cutaneous facial pressure / "skin pull" | Mechanical pressure on face | **PIEZO2 (strong)** |
| Persistent headache, allodynia | Trigeminovascular sensitization | **PIEZO2 plausible; PIEZO1 acute** |
| Vestibular / dizziness / oscillopsia | Vestibular hair cell / afferent | TMC primary; PIEZO2 plausible |
| Cognitive / brain fog | Neurovascular / BBB disruption | **PIEZO1 plausible to strong** |
| Visual / oculomotor | Central brainstem | None |
| Sleep disturbance | Secondary | None |
| Tinnitus | Cochlear injury | PIEZO2 secondary at most |

PIEZO is part of the explanation for some symptoms — particularly the cutaneous, cognitive, and possibly vestibular clusters — but is not the molecular substrate of the defining acute auditory event.

## 4. Two Threat Models, Two Conclusions

The energetics analysis forces us to separate two distinct threat models that the original hypothesis conflated.

### 4.1 Threat Model A: RF Directed Energy (Frey-class)

This is the model implicit in the NAS 2020 report and IC Expert Panel framing.

- **Mechanism:** Pulsed RF → tissue absorption → thermoelastic expansion → bone-conducted acoustic wave → cochlear hair cells.
- **Pressures involved:** 0.02–0.7 Pa in head tissue.
- **Can it activate Piezo channels?** No, by ~10⁴–10⁶. Even at injurious power levels, the gap remains ~50× and the waveform mismatch is severe.
- **Symptoms explained:** Acute directional auditory phenomena. Possibly some headache via direct cochlear/trigeminal nociception.
- **Symptoms not explained:** Cutaneous "skin pull," persistent cognitive deficits, vestibular dysfunction, BBB-related findings.

### 4.2 Threat Model B: Direct Acoustic / FUS-class Exposure

This model is energetically capable of activating Piezo channels but requires a different delivery system than RF.

- **Mechanism:** Focused ultrasound or strong acoustic projection at MHz / sub-MHz frequencies → bulk acoustic pressure 20 kPa to several MPa → direct gating of Piezo1/2 in target tissue.
- **Pressures involved:** 20 kPa to several MPa (a million times higher than RF thermoelastic).
- **Can it activate Piezo channels?** Yes, demonstrably (Hoffman 2022, Zhu 2023, Oh 2019).
- **Symptoms explained:** Cutaneous pressure, vestibular, plausibly cognitive (via cerebrovascular/BBB).
- **Symptoms not explained:** Acute directional auditory click/buzz from a Frey-like microsecond pulse.
- **Operational considerations:** FUS requires acoustic coupling. Through-air FUS at standoff distances is hard. Through-window/through-wall acoustic coupling at the pressures required is non-trivial but not impossible — depends on the specific scenario. This is where the open literature gets thin and the classified domain is opaque.

### 4.3 The reconciled picture

The honest synthesis is that the open AHI literature points strongly to **RF / Frey effect** for the acute auditory event, and that **Piezo-mediated mechanotransduction** has nothing to do with that primary mechanism. But Piezo is a real candidate substrate for the **non-acute, non-cochlear symptom tail** — *if* a different (acoustic) exposure modality is also present, or if RF exposure causes secondary tissue effects (microvascular, BBB) that then engage Piezo1 downstream.

The bridging paper the literature is missing is therefore not a positive "RF→Piezo" mechanism paper. It is:

1. A **negative finding paper** showing the energetic gap rules out the simple RF→Piezo bridge.
2. A **threat-model decomposition paper** separating acute-auditory (RF/Frey) from non-acute symptoms (acoustic/Piezo or RF→secondary→Piezo).
3. A **citation audit paper** documenting that two adjacent fields are not engaging with each other.

## 5. What This Means for the Investigation

### 5.1 What the original hypothesis got right

- The two literatures genuinely don't engage with each other — that pattern is real and structurally interesting.
- DoD funded the FUS-Piezo work (DARPA ElectRx contract HR0011-15-2-0054 to Konofagou produced the [Hoffman 2022 PNAS paper](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2115821119)). That funding trail is real.
- PIEZO2 expression in cutaneous and vestibular afferents is the right molecular fit for several non-cochlear AHI symptoms.

### 5.2 What it got wrong

- The energetics. RF thermoelastic pressures are 10⁴–10⁶ below Piezo gating thresholds. The simple RF→Piezo bridge does not survive a quantitative check.
- The temporal waveform. A Frey pulse is a microsecond transient; Piezo gating wants sustained tension over milliseconds.

### 5.3 What's actually publishable

A short, careful paper titled something like *"Mechanotransduction and Anomalous Health Incidents: A Citation Audit and Energetic Analysis"* that:

- Documents the complete non-citation pattern in both directions (Section 1).
- Shows the RF→Piezo energetic gap quantitatively (Section 2).
- Maps PIEZO1/2 expression to AHI symptom clusters and identifies which ones the channel could be relevant to under what threat model (Section 3).
- Separates the threat models cleanly and identifies which open questions are tractable (Section 4).

This is more valuable as a negative result than the original positive hypothesis would have been if it had survived. It clears the ground for whichever investigators want to pursue threat-model B (acoustic) or RF-secondary mechanisms without the field accidentally rediscovering a wrong simple bridge.

### 5.4 Open questions that remain

- Has anyone tested directly whether an RF thermoelastic transient — even at high power — can gate Piezo channels in a controlled in-vitro preparation? No published experiment found.
- What is the PIEZO2 expression profile in human vestibular afferents specifically? Mouse data exist; human data are thin.
- Are there secondary mechanisms (RF → microvascular injury → mechanical stress → PIEZO1 in BBB endothelium) that bridge the threat models? Not investigated.
- Does the classified IC Expert Panel annex name PIEZO2? Not knowable from open sources.

### 5.5 The funding-trail observation still stands but means less

DARPA paid for the FUS-Piezo work, and the work is real. But the energetic analysis means that the FUS-Piezo work is not a hidden weapons program disguised as therapeutic neuromodulation. It is therapeutic neuromodulation, and its operational threat-model relevance — if any — is to a different threat than RF-based AHI.
