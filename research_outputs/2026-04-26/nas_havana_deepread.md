# NAS Havana Deep-Read: Close Analysis of the 2020 National Academies Report and Subsequent Intelligence Assessments

*Prepared as a close-read analysis. All page numbers reference the NAS 2020 report as paginated in the National Academies Press edition (doi:10.17226/25889). Chapter-level citations use NCBI book IDs where page numbers are approximated from chapter structure.*

---

## Executive Summary

In December 2020, the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine (NASEM) published "An Assessment of Illness in U.S. Government Employees and Their Families at Overseas Embassies" — the most authoritative scientific review of the "Havana Syndrome" cases. The report concluded that **directed, pulsed radiofrequency (RF) energy is the most plausible mechanism** to explain the acute phase of illness among affected State Department employees. It identified the Frey (thermoelastic) effect as the leading candidate for the auditory component, while acknowledging that the mechanism by which pulsed RF energy produces vestibular dysfunction, cognitive impairment, visual disturbances, and other non-cochlear symptoms remains fundamentally unresolved.

Key finding: the report's mechanism inventory stops at the level of thermoelastic pressure wave generation and cochleo-vestibular coupling. It does not address Piezo-channel or TRPV-channel mediated mechanotransduction, vascular endothelial effects, blood-brain barrier disruption, or focused ultrasound-specific biology. These represent a significant explanatory gap — particularly for the chronic, non-cochlear symptom cluster.

The 2022 IC Experts Panel (ODNI) concurred that pulsed electromagnetic energy plausibly explains the "core characteristics," and ultrasound was plausible in close-access scenarios. The 2023 ODNI ICA reversed course institutionally, assessing foreign adversary involvement as "very unlikely." The 2024 update (January 2025) preserved that majority view but saw two IC components shift assessments based on new evidence of foreign RF weapons R&D progress — with one component now assessing it "likely" a foreign actor has an RF antipersonnel capability.

## 1. Full Quotation of the Report's Mechanism Findings on Pulsed RF / Microwave

The NAS 2020 report devoted Chapter 5 ("Plausible Mechanisms") to mechanism analysis. The following are direct quotations from that chapter, as published on NCBI ([NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/)):

### 1.1 Opening Framing

> "The committee felt that these acute symptoms were more consistent with a directed radio frequency (RF) energy attack, and explored possible related mechanisms. At the same time, the chronic symptoms that were reported are often seen in patients after head trauma, as a result of chemical exposure, infectious diseases, or stress in a hostile environment."

[NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/)

### 1.2 Scope of RF Biological Effects Considered

> "Observations from clinicians (including published summaries of symptoms and experiences) and DOS personnel were considered with respect to known biological effects of a wide variety of RF exposures (defined as 30KHz–300GHz, including microwave radiation as 300MHz–300GHz)."

[NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/)

### 1.3 Thermal vs. Non-Thermal Effects

> "Low-level RF exposures typically deposit energy below the threshold for significant heating (often called 'nonthermal' effects), while high-level RF exposures can provide enough energy for significant heating ('thermal' effects) or even burns, and for stimulation of nervous and muscle tissues ('shock' effects)."

[NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/)

### 1.4 Finding on Acute Symptom Consistency with RF

> "Based on a review of these information sources, the committee finds that many of the acute, early phase symptoms and observations reported by DOS employees are consistent with RF effects, including a perceived clicking sound within the head even when the ears were covered, a perceived force/pressure sensation within the head and on the face, perceived spatial localization and directionality of these perceived phenomena and other loud sounds, hearing loss, tinnitus, impaired gait and loss of balance, as well as the absence of heating sensation and absence of observed disruption of electronic devices in the immediate environment. In addition, many of the chronic, nonspecific symptoms are also consistent with known RF effects, such as dizziness, headache, fatigue, nausea, anxiety, cognitive deficits, and memory loss."

[NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/)

### 1.5 Non-Thermal Mechanisms Listed

> "There are multiple possible mechanisms for non-thermal RF biological effects, including apoptosis and cell oxidative stress... RF-induced, non-thermal cell membrane dysfunction can occur from coherent excitation above 1 GHz due to a variety of effects including electroporation, metabolic changes, pressure fluctuations, and voltage gated calcium channel disruption. However, many of the cognitive, vestibular, and auditory effects observed in DOS personnel are most consistent with modulated, or pulsed, RF biological effects."

[NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/)

### 1.6 The Pulsed RF Research Record (USSR/Russia)

> "There was significant research in Russia/USSR into the effects of pulsed, rather than continuous wave (CW) RF exposures because the reactions to pulsed and CW RF energy at equal time-averaged intensities yielded substantially different results. According to Pakhomov and Murphy, the Russian-language studies 'indicated that pulsing may be an important (or even the most important) factor that determines the biological effects of low-intensity RF emissions.' Military personnel (in Eurasian communist countries) exposed to non-thermal microwave radiation were said to have experienced headache, fatigue, dizziness, irritability, sleeplessness, depression, anxiety, forgetfulness, and lack of concentration, as well as internal sound perception for frequencies between 2.05–2.50 GHz."

[NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/)

### 1.7 The Frey Effect (Microwave Auditory Effect)

> "In 1961, Alan Frey identified a new, RF-induced auditory phenomenon in both normal and deaf humans that became known as the 'Frey effect.' The areas near the ear were most sensitive to these RF exposures; modulating the RF energy could produce a variety of effects including the perception of 'buffeting of the head' or pressure on the face/head without dizziness or nausea, a 'pins and needles sensation,' and a sound described as a 'buzz, clicking, hiss, or knocking' within the head for RF frequencies between 0.4–3 GHz, depending on pulse width, pulse-repetition frequency (PRF), and peak power density."

[NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/)

> "Importantly, the Frey effect may be induced without causing identifiable structural injury to neural or labyrinthine tissues."

[NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/)

### 1.8 Vestibular Stimulation Mechanism — The Report's Key Uncertainty

> "The potential for RF sources to stimulate the vestibular end organs via thermoelastic pressure waves (see Appendix C) or to excite central nervous system pathways via transduction akin to the Frey effect **is not known**. However, if these effects exist, this unusual form of vestibular stimulation could lead to very confusing perceptions, as central vestibular pathways do their best to resolve the non-physiological pattern of end organ stimulation resulting in perceptions of physically impossible motions, unexpected reflexive postural responses to them, and faulty inferences about external forces causing them."

[NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/) (emphasis added)

### 1.9 Overall Conclusion

> "The committee finds that many of the acute, sudden-onset, early phase signs, symptoms and observations reported by DOS employees are consistent with RF effects. In addition, many of the chronic, nonspecific symptoms are also consistent with known RF effects, such as dizziness, headache, fatigue, nausea, anxiety, cognitive deficits, and memory loss. It is not necessary for RF energy sources to produce gross structural damage to cause symptoms. Rather, as with the Frey effect or potential thermoelastic pressure waves, RF sources may trigger symptoms by transiently inducing alterations in brain functioning."

[NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/)

### 1.10 Insufficient Data Acknowledgment

> "While there are several studies on the health effects of continuous wave and pulsed RF sources, there are insufficient data in the open literature on potential RF exposure/dosage characteristics and biological effects possible for DOS scenarios. Specific experiments would be needed with RF exposure and dosage characteristics... to quantify the biological effects, but would be ethically difficult to justify. In the absence of such data, it is difficult to align specific biophysical effects within the potential RF exposure regime that could explain specific medical symptoms reported by DOS personnel."

[NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/)

## 3. Non-Cochlear Symptoms Gap Analysis

### 3.1 Vestibular Dysfunction

The NAS report's mechanism for vestibular dysfunction is the thermoelastic pressure wave propagating from cranial tissue to the vestibular end organs (saccule, utricle, semicircular canals). The report explicitly states this "is not known" to occur ([NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/)). The clinical data compound this uncertainty: the Miami group found reduced vestibular evoked myogenic potentials (suggesting peripheral otolith damage), while the Dalhousie group found *elevated* amplitudes — opposite results that the report could not reconcile. The committee concluded: "the aggregate data derived from the subset of well-established clinical laboratory diagnostic tests...did not identify a common pattern of structural injuries to the labyrinths or brains of patients that could explain the reported vestibular symptoms" ([NAS 2020, Ch. 3](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566404/)).

**Gap:** The report cannot explain how vestibular dysfunction arises independently of cochlear trauma, or why it persists when cochlear function normalizes. A central (brainstem/cortical) vestibular disruption pathway via direct RF action on vestibulo-thalamic-cortical circuits is invoked but not mechanized.

### 3.2 Cognitive Impairment

The report acknowledges that cognitive deficits (memory, concentration, executive function) were documented in a majority of patients. It associates these with (a) diffuse forebrain dysfunction ("more consistent with diffuse involvement of forebrain structures and function, such as cerebral cortex or limbic structures") and (b) general pulsed-RF CNS effects as documented in Soviet-era research. The proposed mechanism — transient alteration of brain functioning via Frey-like transduction or thermoelastic waves — does not explain the **persistence** of cognitive deficits months to years after the acute event.

**Gap:** The report acknowledges that "definitive causes of chronic symptoms have not been identified in most DOS personnel with ongoing morbidity" ([NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/)). No mechanism for why transient RF exposure would produce lasting cognitive impairment is proposed. The report attributes some chronic cases to PPPD (a functional vestibular disorder), but PPPD itself does not fully explain the cognitive symptoms.

### 3.3 Headache

The report lists headache as a chronic symptom consistent with "known RF effects," citing Soviet military data on personnel exposed to 2.05–2.50 GHz microwave radiation who "experienced headache, fatigue, dizziness, irritability, sleeplessness, depression, anxiety, forgetfulness, and lack of concentration" ([NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/)). No distinct intracranial mechanism for headache is proposed beyond this epidemiological consistency.

**Gap:** The report does not propose whether headache arises from meningeal nociception, vascular changes, cortical spreading depression, or other specific pathway. The comparison with vestibular migraine cohorts (where headache+vestibular symptom overlap was substantial) is not addressed.

### 3.4 Visual Disturbances

Visual disturbances were reported by 14 of 21 Penn patients — a strikingly high rate (67%). The report notes that the Penn imaging study found "differences in functional connectivity in auditory and visuospatial networks" and decreased diffusivity in the inferior cerebellum ([NAS 2020, Ch. 3](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566404/)). However, the report proposes **no mechanism** for how RF energy would specifically disrupt visual processing circuits.

**Gap:** Visual disturbances are acknowledged but entirely unexplained mechanistically. Neither the Frey effect nor thermoelastic pressure waves are linked to the visual cortex or oculomotor pathways.

### 3.5 Fatigue

Fatigue (10/21 Penn) is categorized as consistent with "known RF effects" and attributed broadly to sequelae of the initial insult or PPPD burden. No specific mechanism is proposed.

**Gap:** The report does not distinguish between fatigue arising from sleep disruption (secondary) versus a primary RF-induced metabolic or neural effect. The Soviet-era literature cited links fatigue to "non-thermal microwave radiation" exposure, but the biophysical mechanism within that literature is not detailed.

### 3.6 Tinnitus

Tinnitus is attributed to cochlear involvement — the thermoelastic acoustic wave causing inner-ear trauma consistent with acoustic overstimulation. In this sense, tinnitus is the symptom *most* explicable by a cochlear pathway.

**Gap:** The report does not address whether tinnitus in these patients has electrophysiological features distinct from classic noise-induced or cochlear-damage tinnitus (e.g., whether audiometric profiles, otoacoustic emissions, or neural synchrony patterns differentiate the Havana cases from standard cochlear tinnitus). The 2022 IC Experts Panel noted "a dearth of systematic research on the effects of the relevant electromagnetic signals on humans" ([2022 IC Experts Panel](https://www.dni.gov/files/ODNI/documents/assessments/2022_02_01_AHI_Executive_Summary_FINAL_Redacted.pdf)).

### 3.7 Cutaneous / Somatosensory Effects (Pressure, Tingling, Burning Without Burn)

The Frey (1962) papers documented that modulated RF at ~1.3 GHz produced not only auditory phenomena but also "buffeting of the head," pressure on the face/head, and a "pins and needles sensation" ([NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/)). The report reproduces this observation but proposes no mechanism for how RF energy would activate cutaneous or somatosensory receptors.

**Gap:** The somatosensory effects (tingling, pressure without thermal burn, diffuse head sensations) are reproduced from Frey's observations as descriptive data, not as mechanized pathophysiology. The specific receptor populations involved — Meissner corpuscles, Merkel discs, free nerve endings, or mechanosensitive Piezo/TRPV channels in skin and peripheral nerves — are not discussed anywhere in the report.

## 5. Where a Piezo-Channel-Mediated Mechanism Would Extend the Model

### 5.1 Background: Piezo Channels and Mechanotransduction

Ardem Patapoutian's group (Scripps Research, now Scripps/Howard Hughes) identified Piezo1 and Piezo2 as mechanically activated cation channels expressed across mechanosensitive cell types. Piezo2 is the principal transducer for touch, proprioception, baroreception, and lung stretch; Piezo1 mediates blood-flow sensing and vascular development ([Wu Tsai Neurosciences Institute, 2023](https://neuroscience.stanford.edu/events/piezo-channels-mechanotransduction-sensory-biology-disease-ardem-patapoutian)). Both channels respond to mechanical force as low as ~10 pN — making them among the most mechanosensitive proteins known.

Critically for the AHI context: multiple peer-reviewed studies (2020–2023) have demonstrated that:

1. **Piezo1 mediates ultrasound-induced neuronal responses in vivo.** A 2023 PNAS study demonstrated that conditional Piezo1 knockout in the mouse motor cortex and central amygdala "significantly reduced ultrasound-induced neuronal calcium responses, limb movement, and muscle electromyogram (EMG) responses" ([PNAS, 2023](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2300291120)).

2. **Focused ultrasound excites cortical neurons via mechanosensitive calcium channels.** A 2022 Nature Communications study demonstrated that "focused ultrasound excites cortical neurons via mechanosensitive calcium ion channels," specifically implicating TRPP1/2 and TRPC1 channels, with partial involvement of Piezo1 and TRPC1 ([Nature Communications, 2022](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-022-28040-1)).

3. **Piezo1 is activated by low-intensity pulsed ultrasound (LIPUS).** A 2021 Nature/Bone Research study showed Piezo1 mediates ultrasound-stimulated mechanotransduction in cellular systems ([Nature, 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41413-020-00124-y)).

4. **TRPV1 and Piezo channels interact.** Activation of TRPV1 (a thermosensitive and mechanosensitive channel in dorsal root ganglion neurons and skin) inhibits Piezo1/Piezo2-mediated rapidly adapting mechanosensitive currents, linking thermal, chemical, and mechanical transduction in a common regulatory network ([Science Signaling, 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4527171/)).

### 5.2 What Piezo-Channel Mediation Would Explain That the NAS Report Does Not

| Symptom | NAS Explanation (2020) | Piezo-Channel Extension |
|---|---|---|
| Head pressure / vibration without thermal sensation | Thermoelastic wave → cochlea (mechanism unconfirmed for non-auditory pressure) | Piezo1/2 activation in cranial mechanosensory neurons, periosteal afferents, or meningeal nociceptors by thermoelastic radiation force; does not require cochlear transduction |
| Vestibular dysfunction with inconsistent labyrinthine findings | Thermoelastic wave → vestibular end organs (explicitly "not known"); no CNS mechanism proposed | Piezo2-expressing vestibular hair cells could be directly activated by RF-induced thermoelastic pressure; Piezo1 in brainstem vestibular neurons could be activated by acoustic radiation force, bypassing end-organ damage |
| Cutaneous tingling, "pins and needles," face pressure without burn | Frey (1962) observed it; no receptor mechanism proposed | Piezo2 is the principal mechanoreceptor in skin for touch and proprioception; RF-thermoelastic pressure waves could activate cutaneous Piezo2 channels in Merkel disc and Meissner corpuscle afferents, producing the observed somatosensory phenomena |
| Visual disturbances (67% of Penn cohort) | Forebrain dysfunction postulated; no mechanism | Piezo1 expressed in retinal ganglion cells and vascular endothelium of the retina; mechanical disruption of retinal vasculature or direct optic nerve stimulation by RF-induced thermoelastic waves could explain visual phenomena |
| Cognitive impairment (persistent, months to years) | "Consistent with RF effects"; no lasting mechanism proposed | Piezo1 knockout studies show hippocampal function disruption; repeated Piezo1 activation (during repeated RF exposures) could trigger Ca²⁺ influx cascades leading to synaptic remodeling and persistent cognitive deficits via hippocampal LTP disruption |
| Fatigue | Attributed to PPPD and secondary sequelae | Piezo1-mediated vascular endothelial mechanosensing affects cerebrovascular regulation; disruption could alter cerebral perfusion and produce fatigue via neurovascular coupling failure |
| Blood-brain barrier disruption (noted in IC Experts Panel for ultrasound; unaddressed in NAS report) | Not addressed in NAS 2020 | Focused ultrasound (and by extension RF-thermoelastic waves at sufficient pressure) is an established clinical tool for BBB opening, mediated partly through mechanosensitive endothelial pathways. Transient BBB opening could allow paracellular entry of inflammatory mediators, providing a pathophysiological basis for the chronic symptom cluster |

### 5.3 Sonogenetics and TRPV Channels

The 2022 Nature Communications study identified a path toward "sonogenetic" sensitization — overexpressing TRPC1, TRPP2, or TRPM4 channels to increase neuronal sensitivity to ultrasound ([Nature Communications, 2022](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-022-28040-1)). TRPV4 (expressed in sensory neurons, astrocytes, and vascular endothelium) is mechanosensitive and thermo-sensitive; TRPV1 is thermally and chemically gated. The NAS report cites "voltage gated calcium channel disruption" (Pall, 2013) but does not distinguish between voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels and mechanically-gated channels like TRP or Piezo families — a distinction that is mechanistically critical, because Piezo channels respond to acoustic radiation force (mechanical) while voltage-gated channels respond to electric field gradients.

## 6. 2022 ODNI IC Experts Panel: Comparison with NAS 2020 Conclusions

### 6.1 Where They Converge

| Issue | NAS 2020 | 2022 IC Experts Panel |
|---|---|---|
| Symptom genuineness | Signs and symptoms are real and require explanation | "The signs and symptoms of AHIs are genuine and compelling" |
| Pulsed RF as leading candidate | Pulsed RF "most plausible mechanism" for acute symptoms | "Pulsed electromagnetic energy, particularly in the radiofrequency range, plausibly explains the core characteristics" |
| Psychosocial factors insufficient | Cannot explain acute directional symptoms | "Psychosocial factors alone cannot account for the core characteristics" |
| Information gaps | "Insufficient data in the open literature" | "Information gaps exist"; "dearth of systematic research" |
| Ultrasound | Not independently assessed as a weapon mechanism; mentioned in context of Appendix C acoustic principles | "Ultrasound also plausibly explains the core characteristics, but only in close-access scenarios and with information gaps" |

### 6.2 Where They Diverge

| Issue | NAS 2020 | 2022 IC Experts Panel |
|---|---|---|
| Scope of cases considered | All Havana/China cases; no case-level stratification by "core characteristics" | Focused specifically on cases with four "core characteristics" (acute audio-vestibular, directionality, spatial locality, absence of medical explanation) |
| Ultrasound mechanism | Not independently assessed | Independently assessed as "plausible" for close-access scenarios, with BBB disruption specifically noted |
| Blood-brain barrier | Not mentioned | Explicitly raised: "Ultrasound is used to open the blood-brain barrier in medical procedures" |
| Data access | Committee relied on open-source literature and sanitized clinical summaries | Panel accessed classified information, compartmented program findings, >1,000 classified documents |
| Ionizing radiation | Briefly considered; not found plausible | Explicitly ruled implausible as a core-characteristic explanation |
| Chemical agents | Considered plausible for chronic symptoms (OP/pyrethroid exposure); did not explain acute directional symptoms | Ruled implausible for core characteristics without synergistic stimuli |

### 6.3 The 2023 ODNI ICA Reversal

The March 2023 ODNI Intelligence Community Assessment — produced by seven IC components — concluded that foreign adversary involvement was "very unlikely," with no credible evidence of a sustained foreign weapons campaign ([ODNI, March 2023](https://www.dni.gov/index.php/newsroom/reports-publications/reports-publications-2023/3673-dni-statement-on-the-intelligence-community-assessment-on-ahis)). This represented a sharp divergence from the 2022 IC Experts Panel (which had assessed pulsed RF as a *plausible* mechanism) on institutional grounds — the 2023 ICA addressed attribution, while the 2022 Panel addressed mechanism plausibility. The 2023 ICA did not dispute the biophysical plausibility of pulsed RF effects; rather, it concluded there was no intelligence evidence tying a foreign actor to any specific event.

### 6.4 The 2024/January 2025 Update

The December 2024 updated assessment (published January 10, 2025) reaffirmed the "very unlikely" majority view but recorded important shifts:

> "One component assesses it is 'likely' a foreign actor has an RF antipersonnel capability and that this capability can cause biological effects consistent with some of the symptoms reported as possible AHIs. The other component assesses there is a 'roughly even chance' that a foreign actor has a capability that could have caused some of the experiences reported as possible AHIs."

([ODNI, January 2025](https://www.dni.gov/files/ODNI/documents/assessments/NIC-Unclassified-ICA-Updated-Assessment-AHI-December2024.pdf))

The 2024 update also noted: "The IC is supporting USG-affiliated lab research on whether RF signals can cause bioeffects consistent with those reported as possible AHIs. The majority of results have historically shown no harmful bioeffects; however more recent, limited studies have produced mixed preliminary results."

This trajectory is consistent with emerging mechanistic science on Piezo-channel and TRP-channel mediated RF/ultrasound neuromodulation, which had not been incorporated into any of the government assessments.

## 7. Limits and Acknowledged Gaps in the NAS 2020 Report

The NAS report explicitly acknowledges the following limits:

### 7.1 Absence of Patient-Level Data

> "Individual patient-level data were not provided to the committee... This made it impossible to link specific symptom constellations, physical examination results, and laboratory or imaging test findings within and between affected individuals for diagnostic purposes."

[NAS 2020, Ch. 3](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566404/)

### 7.2 No Baseline Data

The report notes that baseline visual, auditory, and vestibular data were unavailable for affected personnel, making pre-/post-exposure comparison impossible.

### 7.3 Vestibular Mechanism Unknown

> "The potential for RF sources to stimulate the vestibular end organs via thermoelastic pressure waves... is **not known**."

[NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/) (emphasis added)

### 7.4 Insufficient RF Dosimetry Data

> "There are insufficient data in the open literature on potential RF exposure/dosage characteristics and biological effects possible for DOS scenarios."

[NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/)

### 7.5 Ethical Difficulty of Confirmatory Experiments

> "Specific experiments would be needed with RF exposure and dosage characteristics... to quantify the biological effects, but would be ethically difficult to justify."

[NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/)

### 7.6 Clinical Heterogeneity Unexplained

> "Patient clinical heterogeneity could be due to variability of exposure dosage conditions, differences in interpretation of non-physiological vestibular stimuli, and anatomical differences that could influence individual exposure and/or response."

[NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/)

### 7.7 Chronic Symptoms Not Causally Attributed

> "Despite extensive clinical evaluations, definitive causes of chronic symptoms have not been identified in most DOS personnel with ongoing morbidity."

[NAS 2020, Ch. 5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566408/)

### 7.8 Imaging Findings Inconclusive

The Penn group's MRI findings (volume differences, altered diffusivity, connectivity changes) were assessed as having limited individual-level diagnostic value and lacking replication cohorts. NIH imaging found no differences from controls. The committee concluded: "none of the imaging studies performed so far were sufficient to serve as a basis for a case definition or for management of individual subjects" ([NAS 2020, Ch. 3](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK566404/)).

### 7.9 Entire Non-Cochlear Symptom Cluster Unexplained

Most fundamentally: the report's mechanism (thermoelastic wave → cochlear/vestibular end organs, with possible Frey-like CNS transduction) accounts for auditory phenomena and possibly inner-ear vestibular stimulation. It provides **no mechanistic account** for:
- Visual disturbances
- Cognitive decline
- Headache as a primary phenomenon
- Fatigue as a primary phenomenon
- Cutaneous/somatosensory effects (except as documented observations)
- Persistent post-acute symptoms in those without demonstrable structural injury

The report defaults to "consistent with known RF effects" for the chronic, non-cochlear symptoms — a descriptive label, not a mechanistic explanation.
