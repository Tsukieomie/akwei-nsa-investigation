# Biomagnetic Surveillance Evidence Deep Dive

## Executive Summary

The deeper review changes the evidence posture from “no evidence” to a more precise finding: public evidence supports real biomagnetic sensing, defense-funded biomagnetic sensor R&D, and emerging close-range unshielded magnetocardiography, but it does not support a verified operational capability for long-range biomagnetic surveillance of humans. SQUIDs were introduced publicly in 1964 and became important because they were among the world’s most sensitive magnetometers, with direct applications in biomagnetism, magnetoencephalography, and magnetocardiography ([NIST](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2014/03/magnetic-attraction-physicists-pay-homage-squid-50)).

The strongest affirmative source is DARPA’s AMBIIENT program, whose stated goal was to develop gradient magnetic sensors capable of detecting biological signals outside specialized facilities and in Earth’s noisy ambient field ([DARPA AMBIIENT](https://www.darpa.mil/research/programs/atomic-magnetometer-for-biological-imaging-in-earths-native-terrain)). SRI later described an AMBIIENT demonstration of live magnetocardiography outside an expensive shielded room, which is meaningful evidence of defense-relevant biomagnetic sensing progress but not evidence of surveillance or long-range tracking ([SRI International](https://www.sri.com/press/story/sri-led-team-demonstrates-a-magnetic-sensor-that-detects-biomagnetic-signals/)).

The strongest constraint is range. Public magnetocardiography and magnetoencephalography systems still place sensors close to the body, because cardiac magnetic signals are weak and typically measured at about 3 cm above the chest in the 10 to 100 pT range ([Sensors review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11435997/)). Recent human NV-diamond cardiac measurements detected noncontact cardiac magnetic signals, including in shielded and unshielded environments, but the authors averaged hundreds to thousands of heartbeats and emphasized strong noise suppression for practical use outside shielded settings ([arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.18843)).

Ghost Murmur aligns rhetorically with this R&D trajectory because it invokes long-range quantum magnetometry and heartbeat detection, but independent expert coverage treats the 40-mile heartbeat-detection claim as unverified and inconsistent with public physics ([Military.com](https://www.military.com/feature/2026/04/18/ghost-murmur-heartbeat-tracking-tech-has-experts-questioning-laws-of-physics.html)). Science reported that experts said quantum sensors can detect cardiac fields at a few centimeters, not more than 60 km, and quoted Ronald Wakai saying cardiac signals become invisible once distance exceeds about a meter ([Science](https://www.science.org/content/article/did-quantum-sensors-help-find-u-s-pilot-shot-down-iran-experts-doubt-it)).

## Evidentiary Target

“Biomagnetic surveillance” should be reserved for systems that directly detect magnetic fields generated by living physiology, such as cardiac, brain, spinal, or peripheral nerve currents. Magnetocardiography records magnetic fields generated by cardiac electrical activity, while magnetoencephalography records magnetic fields generated by brain activity ([Sensors review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11435997/), [NIMH](https://www.nimh.nih.gov/research/research-conducted-at-nimh/research-areas/research-support-services/meg)).

This distinction matters because several proven remote physiological systems are not biomagnetic. MIT Technology Review reported that the Pentagon’s Jetson prototype uses infrared laser vibrometry to identify people by heartbeat-induced surface motion at 200 meters, which is optical vibration sensing rather than magnetic sensing ([MIT Technology Review](https://www.technologyreview.com/2019/06/27/238884/the-pentagon-has-a-laser-that-can-identify-people-from-a-distanceby-their-heartbeat/)). A peer-reviewed through-wall heartbeat paper used ultra-wideband impulse radar at roughly 65 cm through a wall, which is radar-based motion/phase sensing rather than biomagnetic sensing ([Sensors](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5901946/)).

## Positive Evidence

### Biomagnetic signals are real

SQUIDs were first publicly introduced in a February 1964 journal paper and became important because their superconducting loops with Josephson junctions could detect extremely weak magnetic fields ([NIST](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2014/03/magnetic-attraction-physicists-pay-homage-squid-50)). David Cohen and James Zimmerman first measured the magnetic field from a human heart using a SQUID in late 1969, choosing the heart because it was the easiest biomagnetic signal: large and regular compared with other living signals ([Massachusetts General Hospital Martinos Center](https://www.nmr.mgh.harvard.edu/news/141119/45-years-of-biomagnetism-and-squid)).

Modern MEG demonstrates that biomagnetic brain sensing is mature in clinical and research settings. NIMH describes its MEG system as using 275 SQUID sensors to measure weak magnetic fields of about 10^-14 T outside the head, with sub-millisecond temporal resolution and favorable-condition source localization on the order of 2 to 3 mm ([NIMH](https://www.nimh.nih.gov/research/research-conducted-at-nimh/research-areas/research-support-services/meg)).

SQUID magnetoneurography extends the biomagnetic domain beyond heart and cortex, but it also illustrates operational constraints. A 2024 review says spinal cord and peripheral nerve magnetic fields are only about one-billionth to one-tenth-billionth of Earth’s magnetic field, and the described system uses a magnetically shielded room, a cryostat/Dewar, liquid helium, and close body contact at the neck or waist ([Frontiers in Medical Technology](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11058660/)).

### Defense R&D interest is real

DARPA’s AMBIIENT program is directly relevant because it explicitly targeted biological magnetic signals rather than general quantum computing or D-Wave-style optimization. DARPA stated that AMBIIENT sought novel gradient magnetic sensors that could detect biological signals outside specialized facilities and in Earth’s noisy ambient field, with applications including MEG, MCG, spinal signal detection, mild traumatic brain injury diagnosis, and brain-machine interfaces ([DARPA AMBIIENT](https://www.darpa.mil/research/programs/atomic-magnetometer-for-biological-imaging-in-earths-native-terrain)).

DARPA framed the technical problem as a dynamic-range and shielding problem: lower-performing magnetometers operate comfortably in Earth’s ambient field, while the highest-performing devices such as SQUID and SERF magnetometers generally require highly shielded and expensive special-purpose laboratory facilities ([DARPA AMBIIENT](https://www.darpa.mil/research/programs/atomic-magnetometer-for-biological-imaging-in-earths-native-terrain)). DARPA’s later RoQS program separately emphasized that quantum sensors can measure magnetic fields, gravity, and motion with unmatched precision but suffer degradation from vibration, electromagnetic interference, and other environmental disturbances on defense platforms ([DARPA RoQS](https://www.darpa.mil/news/2025/quantum-sensors-defense-platforms)).

SRI’s AMBIIENT-related demonstration strengthens the R&D case because it reported live magnetocardiography outside an expensive shielded room. SRI described the system as AMBIIENT technology created with Twinleaf LLC and Princeton University, and it framed the application as diagnosing medical conditions such as heart arrhythmias rather than locating or tracking non-consenting targets ([SRI International](https://www.sri.com/press/story/sri-led-team-demonstrates-a-magnetic-sensor-that-detects-biomagnetic-signals/)).

### CIA/IC-adjacent awareness exists

A CIA Reading Room document titled “White Paper on Brain Disorder Phenomenology Using Noninvasive Brain Analysis” discusses a proposed 1000-sensor SQUID biomagnetometer for MEG-style brain disorder research, including thin-film SQUIDs, cryocoolers, close-proximity scalp coverage, beamforming, and clinical applications ([CIA Reading Room](https://www.cia.gov/readingroom/document/cia-rdp96-00789r003300190001-5)). The document describes brain magnetic signals in the 100 to 1000 fT range and emphasizes that Earth’s field is roughly a billion times stronger than brain signals, which reinforces the sensor challenge rather than proving remote surveillance ([CIA Reading Room](https://www.cia.gov/readingroom/document/cia-rdp96-00789r003300190001-5)).

The CIA Reading Room document is best categorized as evidence of intelligence-community visibility into MEG/SQUID medical technology, not evidence of operational biomagnetic surveillance. Its listed uses are medical diagnostics, neuroscience research, treatment feedback, drug evaluation, prosthetics, and personnel screening-style applications, not covert long-range monitoring of people at a distance ([CIA Reading Room](https://www.cia.gov/readingroom/document/cia-rdp96-00789r003300190001-5)).

### Patents show device development, not surveillance

A Genetesis biomagnetic detection patent describes movable optically pumped magnetometer arrays, shielding, body positioning, and chest-proximate magnetocardiography for medical diagnosis ([Google Patents](https://patents.google.com/patent/US20200170528A1/en)). The same patent describes positioning OPMs within about 2 cm of the skin and using shielding to reduce environmental fields, which is incompatible with long-range standoff surveillance as a public technical claim ([Google Patents](https://patents.google.com/patent/US20200170528A1/en)).

A patent on biomagnetic signal detection using quantum detector arrays describes high-temperature SQUID arrays for MEG, MCG, spinal cord, peripheral nerve, animal, and cell measurements, but it emphasizes moving the device close to a stationary subject and reports proximity as close as 20 mm ([Google Patents](https://patents.google.com/patent/US10338157B2/en)). A medical magnetometer patent describes induction-coil magnetocardiography for body-proximate heart measurements in clinical settings, not remote identification or surveillance ([Google Patents](https://patents.google.com/patent/WO2014006387A1/en)).

## Constraints Against Long-Range Biomagnetic Surveillance

The public technical literature consistently points to close-range measurement. A 2024 review reports cardiac magnetic peak amplitudes of 10 to 100 pT at about 3 cm above the chest, and it lists SQUID, OPM, atomic, induction-coil, magnetoresistive, and magnetoelectric sensors as operating from millimeters to centimeters or, in some cases, several decimeters near the body ([Sensors review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11435997/)).

Unshielded progress does not equal long-range progress. A 2023 unshielded MCG system used OPMs and gradiometric cancellation in Earth’s field, but the measurement sensor was fixed on the participant’s chest via a vest and the reference sensor was about 15 cm away from the measurement sensor ([Science Advances](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10058232/)).

NV-diamond progress also remains close-range and noise-limited in the public record. A 2026 preprint demonstrated noncontact human cardiac magnetic measurements with room-temperature NV sensors in shielded and unshielded environments, but the signal traces required averaging from hundreds to thousands of heartbeats and strong noise suppression for practical unshielded use ([arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.18843)).

The sharpest public critique of the Ghost Murmur range claim comes from domain experts. Science reported that Ronald Wakai said cardiac signals become undetectable past about a meter, that Janette Strasburger warned helicopter rotor interference would be severe, and that Dmitry Budker was “extremely skeptical” of the reported long-range NV-diamond heartbeat claim ([Science](https://www.science.org/content/article/did-quantum-sensors-help-find-u-s-pilot-shot-down-iran-experts-doubt-it)). Military.com similarly concluded that the rescue may have involved advanced sensing or intelligence support, but the claim that CIA tracked a heartbeat from 40 miles away remains unverified and inconsistent with public physics ([Military.com](https://www.military.com/feature/2026/04/18/ghost-murmur-heartbeat-tracking-tech-has-experts-questioning-laws-of-physics.html)).

## Adjacent Remote Physiology

The strongest public evidence for remote physiological surveillance is non-biomagnetic. Jetson reportedly used infrared laser vibrometry to detect heartbeat-induced surface motion from 200 meters, required roughly 30 seconds of good return, worked best when the subject was sitting or standing, and needed a cardiac-signature database for identification ([MIT Technology Review](https://www.technologyreview.com/2019/06/27/238884/the-pentagon-has-a-laser-that-can-identify-people-from-a-distanceby-their-heartbeat/)).

Through-wall physiological sensing is also real but radar-based. A 2018 UWB impulse radar study extracted heart rate through walls with subjects no farther than 1 m from the radar, including a main setup with a clay brick wall 50 cm from the antenna and the subject 15 cm behind the wall ([Sensors](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5901946/)).

These adjacent systems are important for the investigation because a real intelligence operation could plausibly be described as “heartbeat tracking” without directly measuring biomagnetic fields. They also create a plausible pathway for public narratives to conflate optical, radar, thermal, RF, beacon, or multisensor exploitation with biomagnetic quantum sensing.

## Recommended Repo Language

Replace the earlier phrasing “not evidence of biomagnetic surveillance” with a narrower statement:

> Public evidence does not verify operational long-range biomagnetic surveillance, but it does show a real biomagnetic sensing lineage: SQUID-enabled MCG/MEG, DARPA AMBIIENT biological magnetometry R&D, CIA Reading Room visibility into SQUID neuromagnetometer proposals, and emerging close-range unshielded MCG/diamond-magnetometer experiments. The strongest evidence supports clinical/research and defense-R&D interest, not a deployed NSA/CIA capability to locate or identify humans at long range by their magnetic heart or brain signals.

This wording preserves the positive evidence without overstating it. It also keeps Ghost Murmur in the correct category: a reported and unverified long-range heartbeat-detection claim that may be an exaggerated description of advanced sensing, a conflation with non-biomagnetic physiology sensing, or an undisclosed capability beyond the public record.

## Bottom Line

The deeper answer is not “there is nothing here.” The deeper answer is that there is a real biomagnetic technology chain, there is defense-funded work explicitly aimed at taking biological magnetic sensing out of specialized facilities, and there is IC-adjacent documentation showing awareness of SQUID brain-imaging technology.

The unresolved gap is deployment and range. Public evidence supports body-proximate MCG/MEG, unshielded close-range prototypes, and non-biomagnetic remote heartbeat sensing; public evidence does not yet verify long-range biomagnetic surveillance of humans.
