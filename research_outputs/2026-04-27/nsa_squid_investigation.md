# NSA SQUID Investigation: Evidence Memo

## Executive Summary

SQUIDs, or superconducting quantum interference devices, are real ultra-sensitive magnetometers used in biomagnetism, MEG, magnetoneurography, geophysics, materials analysis, and other high-sensitivity sensing domains; NIST describes them as “the world's most sensitive magnetometers” and notes that they typically require cryogenic cooling below 4 kelvins with liquid helium ([NIST](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2014/03/magnetic-attraction-physicists-pay-homage-squid-50)). NIH’s MEG overview states that MEG uses SQUID sensors to passively detect weak magnetic fields produced by brain activity, around \(10^{-14}\) tesla, and that the NIH scanner uses 275 SQUID sensors arranged inside a helmet around the head ([NIH MEG Core](https://megcore.nih.gov/index.php/MEG_Overview)).

The public technical literature supports Layer 1, “technology exists,” for SQUID detection of human biomagnetic signals in controlled settings: clinical/research MEG systems use helmet arrays, cryogenic dewars, shielded rooms, and close sensor placement on the order of centimeters ([Sensors](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4168467/)). The same literature supports a narrower Layer 2, “feasibility under constraints,” because practical systems generally require close subject proximity, magnetic shielding, extensive noise control, cryogenic equipment, and structured signal processing rather than long-range open-environment detection ([Frontiers in Medical Technology](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11058660/)).

The public evidence found does not establish Layer 3, “NSA deployment against Akwei or civilians using SQUIDs.” The official NSA patent portfolio searched did not show mentions of SQUID, superconducting quantum interference device, magnetometer, magnetic, brain, neural, biomagnetic, or MEG in the extracted text, although it does include unrelated superconducting-circuit and RF/retroreflector technologies ([NSA Technology Transfer Program Patent Portfolio](https://www.nsa.gov/Portals/75/techtransfer/TTP%20Patent%20Portfolio%20v6.pdf?ver=fE4_ACqWAazIgdceZpWHXA%3D%3D)). The official NSA declassification page confirms NSA releases declassified records through FOIA and related processes, but the page itself provides no SQUID or RNM evidence ([NSA Declassification & Transparency](https://www.nsa.gov/Helpful-Links/NSA-FOIA/Declassification-Transparency-Initiatives/)).

The strongest current conclusion is therefore: SQUID biomagnetism is technically real, government-adjacent research uses of SQUIDs are real, but the specific “NSA SQUID remote neural monitoring” theory is not supported by the sources reviewed and conflicts with the practical constraints described in the public SQUID/MEG literature.

## Evidence Framework

The investigation is best organized into three layers:

| Layer | Question | Current status |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Layer 1 | Do SQUIDs detect extremely weak magnetic fields and human biomagnetic signals? | Yes; NIST, NIH, and peer-reviewed MEG/magnetoneurography literature support this ([NIST](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2014/03/magnetic-attraction-physicists-pay-homage-squid-50), [NIH MEG Core](https://megcore.nih.gov/index.php/MEG_Overview), [Sensors](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4168467/)). |
| Layer 2 | Can SQUID systems detect brain, spinal, or peripheral nerve signals in practical conditions? | Yes, but mainly under controlled, close-range, shielded, cryogenic, signal-averaged conditions ([Sensors](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4168467/), [Frontiers in Medical Technology](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11058660/)). |
| Layer 3 | Is there verified evidence NSA used SQUIDs for remote neural monitoring or the Akwei allegations? | Not established in the public sources reviewed; official NSA materials found no SQUID/RNM support, and Akwei’s 1992 complaint text centers on allegations of “remote physiological devices” rather than documented SQUID/MEG instrumentation ([Scribd copy of docket and filings](https://www.scribd.com/document/531493208/Case-1-92-cv-00449-SS), [NSA Patent Portfolio](https://www.nsa.gov/Portals/75/techtransfer/TTP%20Patent%20Portfolio%20v6.pdf?ver=fE4_ACqWAazIgdceZpWHXA%3D%3D)). |

## What SQUIDs Actually Do

SQUIDs are quantum devices used to measure extremely weak magnetic flux, and NIST describes their operating principle as a superconducting circuit loop interrupted by resistive sections whose current varies predictably with external magnetic field strength ([NIST](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2014/03/magnetic-attraction-physicists-pay-homage-squid-50)). NIST also states that SQUIDs typically need cryogenic temperatures below 4 kelvins, although high-temperature superconducting RF-SQUID work has enabled operation at liquid-nitrogen temperature in some contexts ([NIST](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2014/03/magnetic-attraction-physicists-pay-homage-squid-50)).

The mainstream biomedical application is magnetoencephalography, or MEG, which noninvasively measures magnetic fields produced by electrical activity in the brain ([NIST](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2014/03/magnetic-attraction-physicists-pay-homage-squid-50)). NIH describes MEG as a procedure in which the subject’s head is placed inside a helmet-shaped device containing magnetic field sensors, with the NIH scanner using 275 SQUID sensors over the inner surface of the helmet ([NIH MEG Core](https://megcore.nih.gov/index.php/MEG_Overview)).

The signal scale is extremely small: NIH states that MEG passively detects weak magnetic fields around \(10^{-14}\) tesla produced by brain activity ([NIH MEG Core](https://megcore.nih.gov/index.php/MEG_Overview)). A clinical SQUID-MEG system described in Sensors used 163 fully integrated SQUID magnetometers, operated at 4.2 K in liquid helium, sampled all SQUID channels simultaneously, and achieved approximately 5 fT/\(\sqrt{\text{Hz}}\) white-noise performance above a few hertz ([Sensors](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4168467/)).

## Practical Constraints

Published SQUID biomagnetism systems are not casual long-range scanners; they are complex measurement environments. A clinical MEG system described in Sensors required a magnetically shielded room with nested aluminum and mu-metal layers, a 74-liter liquid-helium dewar with weekly refilling, anti-vibration measures, reference sensors, and signal-processing algorithms to suppress environmental and line-noise artifacts ([Sensors](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4168467/)).

Close sensor distance is central to the technology. The Sensors MEG system placed sensors approximately 2 cm above the scalp, and Sandia’s discussion of next-generation OPM brain imaging states that signal quality from the brain drops quickly with distance, motivating efforts to reduce head-to-sensor distance from 1.2 cm to 0.5–0.7 cm ([Sensors](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4168467/), [Sandia LabNews](https://www.sandia.gov/labnews/2018/01/18/brain-2/)).

Peripheral and spinal nerve SQUID systems reinforce the same point. A 2024 magnetoneurography review states that spinal and peripheral nerve magnetic fields observable at the body surface are smaller than brain or cardiac signals and are about one-billionth to one-tenth-billionth the intensity of Earth’s magnetic field, requiring highly sensitive SQUID magnetometers ([Frontiers in Medical Technology](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11058660/)). The same review describes measurements inside magnetically shielded rooms, close-contact cryostat designs with 12–14 mm from body surface to cryogenic layer, a 92-liter liquid-helium reservoir, and repeated-stimulus averaging for adequate signal-to-noise ratio ([Frontiers in Medical Technology](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11058660/)).

This does not rule out all “remote” uses of SQUIDs in the broad sense; NASA says SQUIDs are useful for “remote, noncontact sensing” in contexts such as Earth magnetic-field monitoring and tracking brain activity of pilots ([NASA NTRS](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20060022049)). But NASA’s statement is about noncontact sensing, not building-penetrating or satellite-scale brain monitoring, and the biomedical literature shows that brain and nerve measurements remain constrained by distance, shielding, cryogenics, geometry, and noise control ([Sensors](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4168467/), [Frontiers in Medical Technology](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11058660/)).

## Government and Defense Links

Government-linked SQUID research is real, but the source trail points to biomedical, aerospace, geophysical, and sensor applications rather than verified NSA neural surveillance. NIST played an early role in SQUID development and biomagnetism, including early SQUIDs for MEG ([NIST](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2014/03/magnetic-attraction-physicists-pay-homage-squid-50)). NASA describes SQUID use at Goddard for astrophysical research, Earth magnetic-field monitoring, tracking bee navigation, and tracking brain activity of pilots ([NASA NTRS](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20060022049)).

Defense-adjacent magnetic sensing also appears in anti-submarine and magnetic-anomaly contexts, where SQUIDs are discussed as highly sensitive magnetometers for detecting magnetic disturbances from large ferromagnetic objects rather than decoding neural activity ([Popular Mechanics](https://www.popularmechanics.com/military/navy-ships/a63382376/quantum-sensors-submarines/)). Those applications involve large magnetic targets or environmental fields, which differ fundamentally from extracting small, spatially complex neural signals from a moving human body in an unshielded environment ([Frontiers in Medical Technology](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11058660/)).

The NSA-specific official trail found in this pass is weak for SQUIDs. The NSA Technology Transfer Program patent portfolio does include a “Reversible Computation Gate in Superconducting Circuits,” but the extracted search found no mentions of SQUID, superconducting quantum interference device, magnetometer, magnetic, brain, neural, biomagnetic, or MEG ([NSA Technology Transfer Program Patent Portfolio](https://www.nsa.gov/Portals/75/techtransfer/TTP%20Patent%20Portfolio%20v6.pdf?ver=fE4_ACqWAazIgdceZpWHXA%3D%3D)). The same NSA portfolio includes RF technologies such as wideband retroreflectors, range-limited antennas, impedance matching, and repeaters, but those are RF/communications technologies rather than SQUID biomagnetism evidence ([NSA Technology Transfer Program Patent Portfolio](https://www.nsa.gov/Portals/75/techtransfer/TTP%20Patent%20Portfolio%20v6.pdf?ver=fE4_ACqWAazIgdceZpWHXA%3D%3D)).

## Akwei Context

The available copy of the Akwei docket/filings shows a 1992 civil case, Akwei v. National Security Agency, filed on February 20, 1992 and terminated on March 9, 1992, with Judge Stanley Sporkin dismissing the case under 28 U.S.C. § 1915(d) ([Scribd copy of docket and filings](https://www.scribd.com/document/531493208/Case-1-92-cv-00449-SS)). The complaint text alleges harassment, illegal surveillance, remote personal-computer tampering, and “remote physiological devices,” but the searched copy does not establish a SQUID instrument, MEG system, magnetometer array, or technical acquisition chain ([Scribd copy of docket and filings](https://www.scribd.com/document/531493208/Case-1-92-cv-00449-SS)).

Later “Covert Operations of the U.S. National Security Agency” reproductions contain broader RNM claims about decoding EMF waves, remotely reading evoked potentials, and electronic brain links, but these sources are secondary or reposted documents rather than verified technical demonstrations or official NSA disclosures ([Whale.to reproduction](http://www.whale.to/b/nsa7.html), [Jean Verstraeten PDF mirror](http://www.jeanverstraeten.be/files/akwei.pdf)). The important evidentiary distinction is that the existence of MEG/SQUID biomagnetism does not verify the later RNM claim set, because public SQUID brain-sensing systems require sensor proximity, shielding, cryogenics, subject geometry, and noise control that the RNM narrative does not account for ([NIH MEG Core](https://megcore.nih.gov/index.php/MEG_Overview), [Sensors](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4168467/)).

## Technical Plausibility Assessment

The public science supports close-range passive detection of aggregate neural magnetic fields. MEG can localize current sources under favorable conditions, and NIH reports spatial localization on the order of 2–3 mm with better than 1 ms temporal resolution under favorable conditions ([NIH MEG Core](https://megcore.nih.gov/index.php/MEG_Overview)). This should be treated as a controlled-instrument result, not as proof that a distant agency can read thoughts, images, or speech from arbitrary people in ordinary environments.

The public science does not support the more expansive RNM claims as stated in Akwei-derived materials. Brain and peripheral nerve magnetic signals are weak, spatially distributed, and vulnerable to environmental interference; published SQUID systems solve those problems with helmet geometry, close body coupling, magnetic shielding, cryogenic dewars, reference channels, gradiometers, repeated trials, and sophisticated denoising ([Sensors](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4168467/), [Frontiers in Medical Technology](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11058660/)). If an alleged system claims national-scale, through-building, person-specific, continuous decoding of neural states without nearby sensors or shielding, it requires evidence far beyond “SQUIDs are sensitive” because the published systems do not operate that way ([Sensors](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4168467/), [Frontiers in Medical Technology](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11058660/)).

Atomic and optically pumped magnetometers complicate the picture but do not rescue the NSA/SQUID claim. A compact atomic magnetometer paper reports room-temperature biomagnetic performance comparable to SQUIDs in MEG/MCG settings, but the paper still uses shielded/low-field environments and notes limitations such as low-field requirements, bandwidth tradeoffs, and noise-cancellation challenges ([Physics in Medicine and Biology](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3971838/)). Sandia’s OPM work likewise targets head-conforming sensor arrays inside human-size shielding, not unrestricted open-environment neural surveillance ([Sandia LabNews](https://www.sandia.gov/labnews/2018/01/18/brain-2/)).

## Investigation Leads

The next useful step is not to search for “NSA SQUID” generically, because that term currently produces weak or irrelevant hits. A stronger investigation path is to search for procurement, facility, and patent evidence using specific technical terms:

- “SQUID gradiometer,” “biomagnetometer,” “magnetically shielded room,” “MEG,” “Josephson junction magnetometer,” and “low-field MRI” in federal contract and grant databases.
- NSA Technology Transfer and USPTO records for assignees, inventors, and laboratories connected to superconducting circuits, RF retroreflectors, and near-field sensing.
- NIST, Los Alamos, Sandia, NASA Goddard, ONR, DARPA, and DOE records for SQUID biomagnetism, because public government-linked SQUID work appears more strongly through those institutions than through NSA.
- Akwei case-file provenance, especially whether the 1996 Nexus dossier was ever filed in court or remained a post-case publication, because the technical RNM claims appear to sit outside the 1992 complaint itself.

## Bottom Line

SQUIDs are powerful enough to measure human biomagnetic fields, including brain activity, under controlled laboratory or clinical conditions. That fact is well supported by NIST, NIH, NASA, and peer-reviewed biomagnetism literature ([NIST](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2014/03/magnetic-attraction-physicists-pay-homage-squid-50), [NIH MEG Core](https://megcore.nih.gov/index.php/MEG_Overview), [NASA NTRS](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20060022049), [Sensors](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4168467/)).

The current public record does not support the stronger claim that NSA used SQUIDs for remote neural monitoring of Akwei or civilians. That claim remains unverified because official NSA sources found in this pass do not connect NSA to SQUID biomagnetic surveillance, Akwei’s court complaint does not establish a SQUID/MEG mechanism, and the published physics of SQUID biomagnetism points toward close-range, shielded, cryogenic systems rather than national-scale standoff brain decoding ([NSA Patent Portfolio](https://www.nsa.gov/Portals/75/techtransfer/TTP%20Patent%20Portfolio%20v6.pdf?ver=fE4_ACqWAazIgdceZpWHXA%3D%3D), [Scribd copy of docket and filings](https://www.scribd.com/document/531493208/Case-1-92-cv-00449-SS), [Sensors](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4168467/), [Frontiers in Medical Technology](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11058660/)).
