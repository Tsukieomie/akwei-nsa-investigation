## Section 3: Banner v. NSA -- Akwei's Dossier as Federal Court Evidence (2011)

**Source:** `[Source PDF: USCOURTS-flnd-3_11-cv-00120-0.pdf`
**Case:** Per Andrew Banner v. National Security Agency, et al.
**Case No.:** 3:11cv120/RV/EMT
**Court:** United States District Court, Northern District of Florida, Pensacola Division
**Filed:** 2011; Report and Recommendation dated June 28, 2011
**Judge:** Magistrate Judge Elizabeth M. Timothy

### Summary

Per Andrew Banner filed a pro se complaint against NSA/Pentagon alleging "brain zapping" of himself and his family in South America in the 1960s. He claimed electronic voices ("Program Bias") told him about it, that his parents were killed by NSA, and sought $7 billion in damages.

The case was recommended for dismissal for lack of subject matter jurisdiction (the Universal Declaration of Human Rights does not provide a private right of action).

### Relevance to the Akwei Investigation

The court's footnote 2 lists the attachments Banner filed with his complaint:
1. Partial copy of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights
2. A 13-page account by Dave Bader about "Modern Human Experimentation/Torture" describing voices in his head beginning in 1993
3. A 24-page printout from American Patriot Friends Network titled "NSA Mind Control and Psyops"
4. Three pages of handwritten notations
5. **A nine-page printout titled "Covert Operations of the U.S. National Security Agency" by John St. Clair Akwei from Nexus Magazine April/May 96**

### What This Demonstrates

1. **Akwei's dossier has been cited as evidence in at least two federal lawsuits** (Akwei's own 1992 filing and Banner's 2011 filing). It has become a reference document for other individuals making similar claims.

2. **Dave Bader's account (1993)** describes the same phenomenon (voices in his head) beginning just one year after Akwei filed his complaint. This is an independent claimant from a different location describing similar experiences.

3. **Both cases were dismissed on procedural/jurisdictional grounds** -- neither court reached the merits of the technology claims. Akwei's case was dismissed by Judge Stanley Sporkin (former CIA General Counsel), and Banner's was dismissed for citing the Universal Declaration of Human Rights rather than a domestic statute.

4. **The pattern of dismissal without merits review** means the technology claims in both cases have never been adjudicated by a federal court on their substance.
