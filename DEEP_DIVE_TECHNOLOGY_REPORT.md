## Part 1: The Sharp Experiment — Voice-to-Skull Is Not Science Fiction

The idea that words can be transmitted wirelessly into a human skull — without any receiver, earpiece, or implant — sounds like the defining delusion of paranoid schizophrenia. It is also a documented scientific fact, demonstrated in a U.S. military laboratory more than half a century ago.

In the early 1970s, the Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) contacted Dr. Joseph C. Sharp, a former director of research in experimental psychology at the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research, and electronic engineer Mark Grove. ARPA wanted answers about the biological effects of microwave radiation — in part because of the discovery that the Soviet Union had been bombarding the U.S. Embassy in Moscow with microwave energy for years. Sharp and Grove assembled what became "one of the best equipped laboratories in the United States for studying biopsychological effects of microwave radiations" at Walter Reed ([Justesen, *American Psychologist*, March 1975](https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/MICROWAVES-AND-BEHAVIOR.pdf)).

The scientific foundation for their work rested on the "Frey effect," named after biophysicist Allan Frey, who in 1961 demonstrated that human beings can "hear" pulsed microwave energy — perceiving hisses, clicks, and pops that seem to originate inside the head. This effect was initially dismissed by most U.S. investigators as an artifact, but was eventually confirmed through controlled animal studies by Nancy King and subsequently replicated by skeptics who visited microwave laboratories and, to their own surprise, heard the pulses ([Justesen, *American Psychologist*, March 1975](https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/MICROWAVES-AND-BEHAVIOR.pdf)). One such skeptic, upon hearing microwave energy while being irradiated, reportedly said: "Well, I can hear the goddam microwaves, but I still don't believe it!"

Sharp and Grove took the Frey effect a critical step further. They recorded nine single-syllable words — the digits one through ten — on tape. They then processed the electrical sine-wave analogs of each word so that each time a sine wave crossed zero reference in the negative direction, a brief pulse of microwave energy was triggered. They then radiated themselves with these "voice-modulated" microwaves ([Justesen, *American Psychologist*, March 1975](https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/MICROWAVES-AND-BEHAVIOR.pdf)).

The result was unambiguous. Sharp and Grove "were readily able to hear, identify, and distinguish among the 9 words." The publication describes the perceived sounds as "not unlike those emitted by persons with artificial larynxes." This was not telepathy, not hallucination, and not science fiction. It was wireless, receiverless speech transmission via modulated microwave energy, demonstrated at a U.S. Army research facility and published in a peer-reviewed journal ([Justesen, *American Psychologist*, March 1975](https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/MICROWAVES-AND-BEHAVIOR.pdf)).

Communication of longer messages — complex words and sentences — "was not attempted because the averaged densities of energy required to transmit longer messages would approach the current 10 mW/cm² limit of safe exposure" ([Justesen, *American Psychologist*, March 1975](https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/MICROWAVES-AND-BEHAVIOR.pdf)). In other words, the limitation was not that the technology didn't work. It worked. The limitation was that scaling it up risked harming the listener.

This limitation did not stop the U.S. military from pursuing weaponization. In 2003–2004, WaveBand Corporation designed the MEDUSA weapon — Mob Excess Deterrent Using Silent Audio — for the U.S. Navy. MEDUSA was based directly on the microwave auditory effect and was intended to raise the auditory sensation to a "discomfort" level sufficient to deter personnel from entering a protected perimeter or temporarily incapacitate individuals. Sierra Nevada Corporation acquired WaveBand in 2005, but reportedly discontinued the project by 2008, "possibly because it may have been shown to permanently damage human brain tissue" ([Wikipedia — MEDUSA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MEDUSA_(weapon))).

The physics of scaling remain contentious. Kenneth Foster, a bioengineering professor at the University of Pennsylvania who published the first research on the microwave auditory effect in 1974, has argued that the MEDUSA concept was fundamentally impractical: "Any kind of exposure you could give to someone that wouldn't burn them to a crisp would produce a sound too weak to have any effect." Fellow researcher Bill Guy calculated that producing sound at 60 decibels — normal conversation level — would require 40 watts per square centimeter of radiation. "That would kill you pretty fast," Guy said. Producing an unpleasant sound at 120 decibels would require 40 million W/cm² of energy ([Gizmodo, 2008](https://gizmodo.com/update-oops-the-medusa-mind-control-ray-gun-will-actu-5029484)).

Yet Foster himself, in a 2021 peer-reviewed paper, acknowledged that "existing microwave systems can produce pulses with sufficient fluence to induce unexpected and perhaps frightening auditory sensations," and that millimeter-wave transmitters in particular "have characteristics that make them more suitable for 'stealth' (not noticed) attacks." He concluded that while the acoustic waves produced at extreme but feasible exposure levels "are likely to fall short of thresholds for damaging the brain," they "conceivably could produce unpleasant audiovestibular disturbances and/or auditory responses" — and crucially, "the capabilities of high-powered microwave sources remain shrouded in classified research programs" ([Foster et al., *Frontiers in Public Health*, 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8733248/)).

The bottom line: voice-to-skull transmission of recognizable words was demonstrated in 1974. The military attempted to weaponize the underlying effect. The physics debate about practical weaponization continues — but it is a debate about engineering feasibility, not about whether the underlying phenomenon is real.

## Part 2: The INSCOM Report — The Military's Own Assessment

If the Sharp experiment established that microwave-to-brain communication was physically possible, a 1998 classified report from the U.S. Army's Intelligence and Security Command (INSCOM) established that the military considered an entire suite of directed energy bioeffects weapons to be operationally real.

The report, titled "Bioeffects of Selected Nonlethal Weapons," was produced as an addendum to the "Nonlethal Technologies — Worldwide" study (document number NGIC-1147-101-98). It was classified SECRET/NOFORN — meaning it was not to be shared with U.S. allies, NATO, or physically taken outside the United States except to secure U.S. Embassy Military Attaché Facilities. It was produced by the Army's National Ground Intelligence Center, the analytical element responsible for coordinated National Intelligence Community assessments of military threat potentials ([Radiation Research Trust](https://radiationresearch.org/bioeffects-of-selected-nonlethal-weapons/)).

The report was declassified in December 2006 through a Freedom of Information Act request ([Radiation Research Trust](https://radiationresearch.org/bioeffects-of-selected-nonlethal-weapons/)). Its contents, as summarized in a subsequent 2009 DIA document, are remarkable in their specificity. The INSCOM analysis described the following systems as real and potential, based on "current known worldwide (including US for benchmarking) research" ([DIA FOIA Electronic Reading Room](https://www.dia.mil/FOIA/FOIA-Electronic-Reading-Room/FileId/237654/)):

| Capability | Technical Description |
|---|---|
| Microwave communication/hearing | Direct wireless transmission of auditory signals |
| RF carrier-wave modulated intracranial "voices" | Through thermoelastic expansion of intracranial spaces at 5 kHz |
| Directed thermal pain | Ability to direct thermal effects including pain, erythema, and second-degree burns |
| Seizure induction | Use of mm waves to instigate cortical surface effects and seizures through delta-wave neocortical neuronal synchronization with high-voltage 100 kV/m nanosecond pulsed 15 Hz ELF stimulation |
| Short-term memory disruption | Use of pulsed microwaves to temporarily interfere with short-term spatial memory |
| Nausea and disorientation | Inducible with audible frequencies at 145 dB at selected frequencies of 100–500 Hz via infrasonic/sonic combinations |
| Loss of consciousness | Muscle spasms and muscle weakness |
| Core body temperature elevation | To above 41°C within 15–30 minutes with VHF at 225 MHz |

The report addressed radiofrequency directed energy, laser and light phenomena, and aural bioeffects as "maturing nonlethal technologies" ([Radiation Research Trust](https://radiationresearch.org/bioeffects-of-selected-nonlethal-weapons/)). As the DIA subsequently characterized the INSCOM document: it described systems that "exist today" with "no doubt that they would work" ([DIA FOIA Electronic Reading Room](https://www.dia.mil/FOIA/FOIA-Electronic-Reading-Room/FileId/237654/)).

The report's classification as SECRET/NOFORN for eight years — from 1998 to 2006 — is itself significant. For nearly a decade, anyone who publicly described these capabilities would have been describing classified technology. They would have sounded delusional. And yet the technology was real, documented, and assessed by the military's own intelligence apparatus.

## Part 3: The DIA AAWSA Document — When a "Paranoid Schizophrenic" Had a Real Classified Document

Perhaps the most philosophically disturbing document in this entire investigation is not about technology at all. It is about the intersection of mental illness, genuine classified information, and the impossibility of distinguishing the two from the outside.

A 2009 report from the Defense Intelligence Agency's Advanced Aerospace Weapon System Applications (AAWSA) program describes the following case. A U.S. citizen who believed he was being targeted by the government "with harassing field effect weapons" walked into an FBI field office carrying a two-page letter addressed to a U.S. Secret Service agent. The letter stated, in part: "Agents of the U.S. Secret Service, as you already know, have been committing very serious crimes against me and other members of my family for a very long time" ([DIA FOIA Electronic Reading Room](https://www.dia.mil/FOIA/FOIA-Electronic-Reading-Room/FileId/237654/)).

He was arrested. At a competency hearing, he testified that "the back of a pair of his shoes were vaporized by an electromagnetic weapon fired at his feet in 2001." He presented the shoes to the court; "it appeared to the court that the heel of the shoe had simply worn out due to ordinary use" ([DIA FOIA Electronic Reading Room](https://www.dia.mil/FOIA/FOIA-Electronic-Reading-Room/FileId/237654/)).

His mental status was evaluated. The government's psychiatrist found him psychotic. At his request, he was examined by a medical professional of his own choosing. The psychologist chosen by the citizen "concurred with the government's psychiatrist that [he] 'is clearly psychotic and ... precisely fits the diagnosis of paranoid schizophrenia'" ([DIA FOIA Electronic Reading Room](https://www.dia.mil/FOIA/FOIA-Electronic-Reading-Room/FileId/237654/)).

But the story did not end there. The citizen had in his possession a document — a document that turned out to be the 1998 INSCOM report on bioeffects of nonlethal weapons, classified SECRET/NOFORN until 2006. The DIA report states plainly: "[He] may have been crazy, and the Government wasn't harassing him, but his document was valid: it was a newly unclassified US Government document that described precisely and accurately the nature of the Field Effects [he] claimed existed" ([DIA FOIA Electronic Reading Room](https://www.dia.mil/FOIA/FOIA-Electronic-Reading-Room/FileId/237654/)).

The DIA report notes that it "remains a bit murky" how the individual was able to obtain the document through FOIA, given that "other 'legitimate' researchers had consistently not been able to obtain it" ([DIA FOIA Electronic Reading Room](https://www.dia.mil/FOIA/FOIA-Electronic-Reading-Room/FileId/237654/)).

The implications of this case are profound and resist easy categorization:

- The technology the man described was real. The government's own classified assessment confirmed it.
- The man was nonetheless genuinely mentally ill, diagnosed as paranoid schizophrenic by both government and independent psychiatrists.
- The government was not, in fact, targeting him with these weapons.
- Yet he possessed a genuine classified document that validated the technological premise of his claims.

This case illustrates a fundamental epistemological problem that runs through the entire Akwei investigation: the existence of a technology does not validate claims of its deployment against specific individuals. A person can be simultaneously correct about what is technologically possible and profoundly wrong about what is actually happening to them. The technology is real. The targeting claims are a separate question entirely.

## Part 4: The NSA's Own Admission — "A High-Powered Microwave System Weapon"

The NSA's own documents move the discussion from laboratory research and military assessments to operational intelligence about hostile-state weapons actually deployed in the field.

In 2014, an unclassified NSA document — dated October 16, 2014 — was revealed through litigation and made public by former NSA counterintelligence official John Schindler. The document confirmed intelligence information about "a high-powered microwave system weapon" held by a "hostile country." The weapon, according to the NSA, "may have the ability to weaken, intimidate or kill an enemy over time and without leaving evidence" ([Washington Times, September 2024](https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2024/sep/5/inside-ring-nsa-document-reveals-russians-had-micr/)).

The document further stated that the weapon was "designed to bathe a target's living quarters in microwaves, causing numerous physical effects, including a damaged nervous system" ([Washington Times, September 2024](https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2024/sep/5/inside-ring-nsa-document-reveals-russians-had-micr/)).

The context was a 1996 case involving two NSA counterintelligence officials — Chuck Gubete and Mike Beck — who were working in Russia when they suffered debilitating brain-related injuries after a suspected microwave attack. Their symptoms included dizziness and diminished cognition — symptoms that would become grimly familiar to U.S. diplomatic personnel in Havana nearly two decades later. Both Gubete and Beck were diagnosed approximately ten years after the incident with unusual, early-onset Parkinson's disease. Gubete died several years ago. Beck lived with Parkinson's disease, which he believed was caused by the brain attack in Russia, until his death in late January 2026 ([Washington Times, September 2024](https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2024/sep/5/inside-ring-nsa-document-reveals-russians-had-micr/); [Grey Dynamics, March 2026](https://greydynamics.com/havana-syndrome-new-evidence-reignites-debate/)).

The hostile country referenced in the NSA document was one "where Mr. Beck traveled in the late 1990s." The NSA concluded there was "no evidence the weapon was used against Mr. Beck" — while simultaneously confirming that the weapon existed and was capable of exactly the effects Beck experienced ([Washington Times, September 2024](https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2024/sep/5/inside-ring-nsa-document-reveals-russians-had-micr/)).

This is a critical data point for the Akwei investigation. The NSA — the very agency Akwei accused of targeting him — confirmed in its own documentation that a foreign adversary possessed a microwave weapon capable of damaging nervous systems through walls, without leaving evidence. The agency that Akwei claimed was deploying directed energy against him was itself documenting that such weapons were real, operational, and had potentially been used against its own personnel.

## Part 5: The Captured Weapon — March 2026

The most dramatic development in the directed energy weapons landscape occurred in 2024 and was revealed to the public in early 2026. It transforms the entire debate from theoretical possibility to physical reality.

According to reporting by CBS News' *60 Minutes* (aired March 8, 2026), confirmed by journalist Sasha Ingber and national security attorney Mark Zaid, undercover agents of the Department of Homeland Security purchased a previously unknown pulsed microwave weapon from a Russian criminal network in 2024. The operation cost approximately $15 million and was funded by the Pentagon ([*60 Minutes* transcript, March 2026](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/us-military-tested-device-that-may-be-tied-to-havana-syndrome-60-minutes-transcript/); [Grey Dynamics, March 2026](https://greydynamics.com/havana-syndrome-new-evidence-reignites-debate/)).

The device, still classified and not seen by reporters, was described by multiple sources with consistent details ([*60 Minutes* transcript, March 2026](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/us-military-tested-device-that-may-be-tied-to-havana-syndrome-60-minutes-transcript/)):

| Characteristic | Description |
|---|---|
| Size | Portable, concealable, small enough to be carried by a person; fits inside a backpack |
| Appearance | Does not look anything like a gun |
| Power | Uses relatively little power |
| Operation | Silent; does not create heat like a microwave oven |
| Programmability | Programmable for different scenarios; operable by remote control |
| Range | Several hundred feet |
| Penetration | Can penetrate windows and drywall |
| Origin | Vital components made in Russia |
| Key technology | Software-driven programming shapes a unique electromagnetic wave that rises and falls abruptly and pulses rapidly |

The device was tested on animals at a U.S. military laboratory for more than a year. Tests on rats and sheep showed "injuries consistent with those seen in humans" — meaning injuries consistent with the brain damage documented in Havana Syndrome cases ([*60 Minutes* transcript, March 2026](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/us-military-tested-device-that-may-be-tied-to-havana-syndrome-60-minutes-transcript/); [CBS News report, March 2026](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/60-minutes-havana-syndrome-report-finds-u-s-government-tested-energy-weapon/)).

Dr. David Relman, a Stanford University professor of medicine who led two government-commissioned investigations into Havana Syndrome, told *60 Minutes* that the captured device uses the technology his panels theorized could inflict the injuries: pulsed microwave radiation. Relman stated that "the large majority of work" on such devices had "been conducted in the former Soviet Union," where researchers found that "effects could range from loss of consciousness to seizures to memory lapses, inability to concentrate, headaches, intense pressure, pain, disorientation, difficulty with balance" ([*60 Minutes* transcript, March 2026](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/us-military-tested-device-that-may-be-tied-to-havana-syndrome-60-minutes-transcript/)).

Relman also addressed the Russian science behind the weapon's design: "What the Russians spoke about was the importance of the energy being pulsed in order to have biological effects on humans. When you produce pulses like this, you can actually stimulate electrically active tissue like brain tissue and the heart, for that matter, mimicking what the brain normally does, but now you're driving it with your pulses from the outside" ([*60 Minutes* transcript, March 2026](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/us-military-tested-device-that-may-be-tied-to-havana-syndrome-60-minutes-transcript/)).

National security attorney Mark Zaid, who represents nearly three dozen federal victims of Anomalous Health Incidents (AHIs), confirmed on social media: "I can confirm that I had the same information of USG possessing one or more such devices — and from different sources" ([Sasha Ingber, Substack, January 2026](https://sashaingber.substack.com/p/exclusive-us-has-been-testing-a-captured)).

Sources told *60 Minutes* that "there are likely many of these devices" and that "if undercover agents could purchase one from gangsters, then the Russians have lost control of a stealth weapon that could be used by anyone, anywhere" ([*60 Minutes* transcript, March 2026](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/us-military-tested-device-that-may-be-tied-to-havana-syndrome-60-minutes-transcript/)).

The revelations about the captured weapon have catalyzed a significant institutional reckoning:

However, two IC elements have notably refused to endorse the "very unlikely" conclusion: the National Security Agency (NSA) and the U.S. Army's Intelligence Command — the two entities with the deepest institutional knowledge of signals intelligence and directed energy research ([Washington Examiner, March 2026](https://www.washingtonexaminer.com/opinion/beltway-confidential/4490516/congress-must-question-intelligence-community-havana-syndrome-cover-up/)).

**Classified evidence.** Additional classified evidence described by *Grey Dynamics* includes video footage showing two FBI agents and their families at a table on vacation: "a man with a backpack walks in, and everyone simultaneously grabs their head as if in pain." A separate video reportedly shows "two people collapsing at the same time while walking toward a secure facility" inside the U.S. embassy in Vienna. ## Part 6: The Skeptic's Case

Intellectual honesty requires presenting the strongest version of the opposing argument. The skeptical case against directed energy weapon attacks is substantial, and the debate remains genuinely unresolved.

Robert E. Bartholomew, a scholar of mass delusion, published a detailed critique in *Skeptic* magazine following the March 2026 *60 Minutes* broadcast. His central arguments merit serious consideration ([Skeptic, March 2026](https://www.skeptic.com/article/selling-fear-half-truths-latest-60-minutes-expose-havana-syndrome/)):

**The intelligence consensus.** Two separate intelligence community assessments — in 2023 and 2025 — concluded that foreign-directed attacks were "very unlikely" as an explanation for AHIs. While critics allege a cover-up, Bartholomew notes that these assessments represent the professional judgment of the majority of U.S. intelligence agencies ([Skeptic, March 2026](https://www.skeptic.com/article/selling-fear-half-truths-latest-60-minutes-expose-havana-syndrome/)).

**Alternative explanations.** The reported symptoms — headaches, dizziness, tinnitus, fatigue, insomnia, cognitive difficulties — are, individually, extremely common. Bartholomew cites data suggesting the average person experiences five such symptoms per week: 36% report fatigue, 35% headaches, and 30% insomnia. He argues that vestibular disorders, migraines, anxiety, pre-existing conditions, and environmental factors can explain many cases ([Skeptic, March 2026](https://www.skeptic.com/article/selling-fear-half-truths-latest-60-minutes-expose-havana-syndrome/)).

**Social contagion.** The initial cases in Havana may have been amplified by what psychologists call social contagion or mass psychogenic illness. Bartholomew points to "patient zero" — an undercover agent in Havana who reportedly lobbied and coerced colleagues to report symptoms. CIA analyst Fulton Armstrong stated: "He was lobbying, if not coercing, people to report symptoms and connect the dots." Sound recordings from Havana were later identified as Indies short-tailed cricket mating calls ([Skeptic, March 2026](https://www.skeptic.com/article/selling-fear-half-truths-latest-60-minutes-expose-havana-syndrome/)).

**Physics constraints.** The gap between laboratory-demonstrated microwave auditory effects and a practical, deployable weapon remains significant. As noted above, Kenneth Foster of the University of Pennsylvania argued that the MEDUSA weapon "would kill you well before you were bothered by the noise" ([Gizmodo, 2008](https://gizmodo.com/update-oops-the-medusa-mind-control-ray-gun-will-actu-5029484)). Bartholomew notes that "laws of physics" remain a "major impediment to producing effective, practical directed energy weapon from tested device" and that there is a "big difference between research/testing and deployable weapon" ([Skeptic, March 2026](https://www.skeptic.com/article/selling-fear-half-truths-latest-60-minutes-expose-havana-syndrome/)).

**The Norwegian experiment.** In February 2026, the *Washington Post* reported that a Norwegian government scientist had built a pulsed-energy device to test the hypothesis on himself, and subsequently suffered neurological symptoms consistent with Havana Syndrome. However, Bartholomew notes that the researcher's identity has not been publicly confirmed, and Norwegian microwave expert Trym Holter called the self-experiment "completely crazy" and noted it would normally require ethics approval and test subjects ([Skeptic, March 2026](https://www.skeptic.com/article/selling-fear-half-truths-latest-60-minutes-expose-havana-syndrome/); [Reuters, February 2026](https://www.reuters.com/business/healthcare-pharmaceuticals/cia-pentagon-reviewed-secret-havana-syndrome-device-norway-washington-post-2026-02-14/); [People, February 2026](https://people.com/scientist-made-havana-syndrome-machine-to-disprove-theories-then-got-sick-11908026)).

**Conflicts of interest.** Bartholomew points out that *60 Minutes* producers Oriana Zill de Granados and Michael Rey are marketing a forthcoming book titled *The Havana Syndrome: Secret Weapons, a Government Cover-up, and the Greatest Spy Mystery of Our Time*, with an introduction by Scott Pelley — potentially compromising the journalistic independence of their reporting ([Skeptic, March 2026](https://www.skeptic.com/article/selling-fear-half-truths-latest-60-minutes-expose-havana-syndrome/)).

**The uncharacterized device.** The captured Russian device exists — this is not disputed. But it has not been publicly characterized. The animal testing results are classified. No independent scientists have examined it. The connection between this specific device and the specific injuries reported by AHI victims remains, for the public record, an assertion based on anonymous sources rather than published evidence ([Skeptic, March 2026](https://www.skeptic.com/article/selling-fear-half-truths-latest-60-minutes-expose-havana-syndrome/)).

The skeptical case deserves weight. But it also has vulnerabilities. It must account for the biomarker evidence (neurofilament light chain levels three standard deviations above normal), the video evidence described in classified briefings, the GRU Unit 29155 geolocation data linking Russian operatives to incident sites, and the fact that the two IC elements with the deepest signals intelligence expertise — the NSA and Army Intelligence Command — have declined to endorse the "very unlikely" conclusion ([Washington Examiner, March 2026](https://www.washingtonexaminer.com/opinion/beltway-confidential/4490516/congress-must-question-intelligence-community-havana-syndrome-cover-up/); [Grey Dynamics, March 2026](https://greydynamics.com/havana-syndrome-new-evidence-reignites-debate/)).

The debate is not resolved. But the ground has shifted dramatically beneath the skeptics' feet.

## Part 7: What This Means for the Akwei Claims

Three separate investigations into the claims made by John St. Clair Akwei against the National Security Agency can now be synthesized against a technological landscape that has changed fundamentally since the first investigation began. The findings fall into two starkly different categories: what has been confirmed, and what remains unproven.

### What Is Confirmed

**1. Voice-to-skull transmission of recognizable words was demonstrated in 1974 at a military laboratory.**

Dr. Joseph C. Sharp and Mark Grove, working at the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research under ARPA sponsorship, successfully transmitted nine spoken words into the human head using modulated microwave pulses, without any receiver or implant. This was published in *American Psychologist* in March 1975. The technology is real ([Justesen, *American Psychologist*, March 1975](https://zoryglaser.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/MICROWAVES-AND-BEHAVIOR.pdf)).

**2. The U.S. military's own classified 1998 report assessed that directed energy bioeffects weapons "exist today" and "there is no doubt they would work."**

The INSCOM report NGIC-1147-101-98, classified SECRET/NOFORN until 2006, documented the feasibility of microwave hearing, RF-modulated intracranial voices, directed thermal pain, seizure induction, short-term memory disruption, and nausea/disorientation through directed energy ([DIA FOIA Electronic Reading Room](https://www.dia.mil/FOIA/FOIA-Electronic-Reading-Room/FileId/237654/); [Radiation Research Trust](https://radiationresearch.org/bioeffects-of-selected-nonlethal-weapons/)).

**3. The NSA confirmed intelligence of a hostile-country microwave weapon capable of damaging nervous systems without leaving evidence.**

A 2014 unclassified NSA document described "a high-powered microwave system weapon" that was "designed to bathe a target's living quarters in microwaves, causing numerous physical effects, including a damaged nervous system" ([Washington Times, September 2024](https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2024/sep/5/inside-ring-nsa-document-reveals-russians-had-micr/)).

**4. A portable, concealable pulsed microwave weapon was purchased by the U.S. government from a Russian criminal network and tested on animals in 2024.**

The device — portable, silent, programmable, operable by remote control, with a range of several hundred feet and the ability to penetrate windows and drywall — produced injuries in rats and sheep consistent with those seen in Havana Syndrome cases ([*60 Minutes* transcript, March 2026](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/us-military-tested-device-that-may-be-tied-to-havana-syndrome-60-minutes-transcript/); [Grey Dynamics, March 2026](https://greydynamics.com/havana-syndrome-new-evidence-reignites-debate/)).

**5. The DIA documented a case where a diagnosed schizophrenic possessed a genuine classified document about the exact technology he claimed existed.**

The man was "clearly psychotic" by dual psychiatric assessment, the government was not targeting him, but his document was "valid" — a genuine SECRET/NOFORN INSCOM assessment that "described precisely and accurately the nature of the Field Effects" he claimed existed ([DIA FOIA Electronic Reading Room](https://www.dia.mil/FOIA/FOIA-Electronic-Reading-Room/FileId/237654/)).

### What Remains Unproven

**None of this confirms Akwei's specific claims about NSA targeting of individual U.S. citizens.**

The existence of directed energy weapons — even weapons in the hands of the U.S. government — does not establish that the NSA deployed them against John St. Clair Akwei or any other specific domestic target. The Akwei dossier describes a program of systematic targeting of a private citizen by an intelligence agency. No declassified document, congressional finding, or journalistic investigation has corroborated the existence of such a domestic targeting program.

**None of this confirms "Remote Neural Monitoring" as Akwei described it.**

Akwei's most extraordinary claim — that the NSA can remotely read the thoughts and neural activity of individual citizens through their unique bioelectric signatures — remains entirely unsupported. The technology gap between "causing symptoms at range" (demonstrated) and "reading brain activity remotely" (not demonstrated) is vast. Transmitting energy into a head is a fundamentally different physical problem from detecting and decoding the brain's own minute electrical signals at a distance. No peer-reviewed research, declassified document, or credible source supports the claim that any entity possesses this capability.

**The technology gap between directed energy effects and remote neural monitoring remains insurmountable by public evidence.**

Pulsed microwave weapons work by delivering energy to biological tissue. Remote neural monitoring would require detecting and interpreting the brain's endogenous electromagnetic emissions — signals that are orders of magnitude weaker than anything detectable at range with known technology. As Foster noted in his 2021 paper, even the demonstrated microwave auditory effect requires "large antenna" systems and produces exposures that "would make the presence of such a transmitter very obvious" at lower frequencies ([Foster et al., *Frontiers in Public Health*, 2021](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8733248/)). Reading brain states remotely is a qualitatively different and far more demanding proposition than inducing effects in brain tissue.

**The pattern of TI litigation demonstrates how real technology concepts get woven into unfounded individual targeting claims.**

The DIA's own case study — the man diagnosed with paranoid schizophrenia who possessed a real classified document — is the perfect illustration. The technology was real. The targeting was not. Across the broader "Targeted Individual" community, real scientific phenomena (the Frey effect, directed energy bioeffects, microwave hearing) serve as the factual kernel around which elaborate and unfounded personal targeting narratives crystallize. The pattern — real technology, false attribution of personal targeting — recurs with remarkable consistency.

**The Akwei dossier's internal evidence does not require directed energy to explain.**

Any correlations between Akwei's complaints and real events (such as the death or employment changes of named individuals) are consistent with conventional explanations — coincidence, conventional surveillance, or conventional harassment — and do not require electromagnetic weapons to explain. Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence, and the specific operational claims in the Akwei dossier (NSA satellites tracking individual neural signatures, remote thought-reading, etc.) remain far beyond anything documented in the public record.

### Final Assessment

The technology landscape has shifted dramatically since the first Akwei investigation. What was once easily dismissable as paranoid fantasy — that the government possesses weapons that can transmit sounds into heads, cause brain injuries at range, and operate through walls without leaving evidence — is now documented fact. Voice-to-skull is real. Directed energy weapons that cause brain injury at range exist. They are in the hands of state actors and, apparently, non-state actors. The U.S. government has acquired and tested one.

The DIA case study stands as a permanent warning against epistemic arrogance: a man can be genuinely mentally ill and simultaneously in possession of genuine classified information about real technology. The psychiatrists were right about the diagnosis. The intelligence analysts were right about the document. Both truths coexisted in the same person.

But the specific claims in the Akwei dossier about remote thought-reading, neural monitoring via satellite, and systematic NSA targeting of individual American citizens remain unsupported by any evidence uncovered across three investigations. The pattern observed across the TI community — including individuals like Moore, Banner, Caterbone, Starrett, and Byars — consistently shows how real technology concepts become incorporated into shared persecutory belief systems that demonstrate classic features of delusional thinking.

The honest conclusion is uncomfortable in its ambiguity: the technology is far more real than mainstream skeptics acknowledged for decades. The institutional cover-up of Havana Syndrome — if that is what it was — damaged the credibility of official denials. The captured weapon exists. The brain injuries are documented. But none of this validates the specific, extraordinary claims of the Akwei dossier. The gap between "a weapon exists that can cause brain injury through walls" and "the NSA is reading my thoughts via satellite" remains as wide as ever.

The technology is real. The targeting claims remain unproven.
