# 27. The Afterlife of the Akwei Dossier

> How a dismissed 1992 federal complaint became the canonical text of the "targeted individual" community — and kept appearing in federal courts for three decades.

---

## 1. Introduction

In April/May 1996, John St. Clair Akwei published "Covert Operations of the U.S. National Security Agency" in [*Nexus Magazine*](https://nexusmagazine.com/product/covert-mind-control-operations-of-the-nsa/) (Vol. 3, No. 3). The article was a reformatted version of his 1992 federal complaint (*Akwei v. NSA*, [Civil Action 92-0449](https://www.scribd.com/document/531493208/Case-1-92-cv-00449-SS)), which Judge Stanley Sporkin had dismissed in 17 days.

The *Nexus* article described, in technical detail:
- NSA "Signals Intelligence" domestic operations
- "Remote Neural Monitoring" (RNM) — the alleged ability to decode brainwave patterns via satellite
- "Electronic Brain Link" (EBL) technology
- Specific NSA organizational structures and personnel
- Named neighbors (the Kinnecome family at 814 Malta Lane) as NSA operatives

The article was never retracted, corrected, or challenged by the NSA. Over the following three decades, it became the single most cited document in the "targeted individual" (TI) community — a shared explanatory framework referenced in federal lawsuits, FOIA requests, online forums, and at least one government intelligence fusion center's own files.

This investigation file documents the dossier's verified appearances in the federal court system and government records.

### Case Details

#### Moore v. Bush (2007)

John E. Moore filed suit in the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia against President George W. Bush and others, alleging government surveillance and harassment. Moore attached a complete 14-page printout of the Akwei *Nexus Magazine* article as supporting evidence for his claims. The case was dismissed. This is the earliest confirmed federal court filing to incorporate the Akwei dossier as an exhibit — 15 years after the original complaint and 11 years after the *Nexus* publication. [CaseMine](https://www.casemine.com/judgement/us/5914681fadd7b049342bef97/amp)

#### Banner v. NSA (2011)

Andrew Banner filed suit in the Northern District of Florida directly against the NSA. Banner attached a 9-page version of the Akwei dossier as a court exhibit. The case followed the same pattern: pro se plaintiff, claims of electronic harassment and surveillance, Akwei material as evidentiary foundation, dismissal. [GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/USCOURTS-flnd-3_11-cv-00120/pdf/USCOURTS-flnd-3_11-cv-00120-0.pdf)

#### Caterbone v. NSA (2016–2020)

A serial filer in the Eastern District of Pennsylvania, Caterbone brought multiple actions against the NSA and other intelligence agencies over a four-year period. The complaints incorporated the Akwei framework extensively, including "remote neural monitoring" terminology and the organizational structure described in the *Nexus* article. Multiple petitions for certiorari to the U.S. Supreme Court were denied.

#### Byars v. US Government (2014)

Filed in the District of Idaho, Byars alleged "remote neural monitoring" by the U.S. government — terminology that traces directly to the Akwei dossier. The case was dismissed.

#### Starrett v. United States (2021)

William Henry Starrett Jr. filed an action in the U.S. Court of Federal Claims seeking $10.98 trillion in damages for alleged "Remote Neural Monitoring." The use of the specific capitalized term "Remote Neural Monitoring" — coined by Akwei — demonstrates the dossier's continued influence nearly 30 years after the original complaint. [Court of Federal Claims ECF](https://ecf.cofc.uscourts.gov/cgi-bin/show_public_doc?2021cv1168-22-0)

## 3. The Washington State Fusion Center Incident (2018)

In April 2018, investigative journalist Curtis Waltman filed a FOIA request with the Washington State Fusion Center (also known as the Washington State Intelligence Fusion Complex). The responsive documents, [reported by MuckRock](https://www.muckrock.com/news/archives/2018/apr/18/fusion-center-em/), included materials referencing:

- "Remote neural monitoring"
- "Psycho-electronic" weapon effects
- Diagrams and descriptions consistent with the Akwei dossier framework

The significance of this release is threefold:

1. **Government possession**: The Akwei-derived material was found *within government intelligence files*, not merely in civilian court filings.
2. **Fusion center context**: State fusion centers are joint federal-state intelligence sharing hubs. The presence of this material suggests it had circulated through official or semi-official channels.
3. **Accidental disclosure**: The material was released inadvertently, suggesting it was filed alongside legitimate intelligence products rather than being deliberately curated for public release.

The fusion center did not explain how the material entered its files. [MuckRock](https://www.muckrock.com/news/archives/2018/apr/18/fusion-center-em/)

## 4. The Pattern

Every federal case incorporating the Akwei dossier shares the same characteristics:

| Feature | Pattern |
|---------|---------|
| **Filing status** | Pro se (self-represented) |
| **IFP status** | In forma pauperis (or equivalent) |
| **Claims** | Electronic harassment, surveillance, "remote neural monitoring" |
| **Evidentiary basis** | Akwei *Nexus* article (full or partial) |
| **Named defendants** | NSA, U.S. Government, and/or intelligence agencies |
| **Outcome** | Dismissed (typically under § 1915 or Rule 12) |
| **Appeal outcome** | Affirmed or certiorari denied |

This pattern has repeated at least five times across four federal judicial districts and one appellate court over a 14-year span (2007–2021). The template is remarkably consistent:

1. Individual experiences symptoms they attribute to electronic harassment
2. Individual discovers the Akwei *Nexus* article online
3. The article provides a technical-sounding explanatory framework ("Remote Neural Monitoring," "Electronic Brain Link," specific NSA programs)
4. Individual files federal complaint incorporating Akwei terminology and attaching the article
5. Case is dismissed as frivolous

The Akwei dossier functions not as evidence but as a **shared explanatory framework** — a document that gives isolated individuals a common vocabulary and institutional target for their experiences.

## 5. What This Means for the Akwei Investigation

The dossier's afterlife raises several questions that are central to this investigation:

### 5a. The Credibility Problem

The Akwei dossier's adoption by the TI community has created a recursive credibility problem. Courts dismiss TI claims as delusional, citing the Akwei material as evidence of shared delusion. But several of Akwei's core technical claims have been independently validated:

- **Microwave auditory effect**: Confirmed by Sharp/Grove at Walter Reed (1974), weaponized as MEDUSA (2003–2004)
- **Directed energy bioeffects**: Confirmed by INSCOM report NGIC-1147-101-98 (1998, declassified 2006)
- **Domestic surveillance capabilities**: Validated by Snowden disclosures (2013)
- **Pulsed microwave weapons**: Confirmed by National Academies (2020), 60 Minutes weapon capture (2024/2026)

The fact that the document is used by individuals with likely mental health conditions does not, by itself, establish that all claims in the document are false. The [DIA AAWSA report](https://www.dia.mil/FOIA/FOIA-Electronic-Reading-Room/FileId/237654/) documented a case where a diagnosed schizophrenic possessed a genuine classified INSCOM document — demonstrating that mental illness and access to real classified information are not mutually exclusive.

### 5b. The Dissemination Question

How did the Akwei dossier reach the Washington State Fusion Center's files? Two possibilities:

1. **Monitoring pathway**: Federal agencies monitor TI community communications and the Akwei material was collected as part of that surveillance — meaning the government was tracking the document's spread.
2. **Analytical pathway**: An analyst at the fusion center independently sourced the material as reference for understanding TI-related threat reports — meaning government analysts found the document substantive enough to file.

Neither explanation is benign from the NSA's perspective.

### 5c. The Volume Signal

Five confirmed federal court filings (and likely many more unidentified) over 14 years, all following the same template, all citing the same source document. This volume suggests:

- The Akwei dossier is the **single most influential document** in TI community legal action
- It provides a **template effect** — lowering the barrier to filing by giving plaintiffs pre-formatted technical language
- The pattern of filings creates **judicial precedent** for dismissal, which then applies to any future complaint using similar language — including potentially legitimate ones
