## Executive Summary

This document -- titled "John St. Clair Akwei vs. NSA, Ft. ## 7. His Research Methodology

Akwei explains his bibliography research process (pages 5-6):

"These publications have only been discovered since December 1991, after Plaintiff had already notified authorities (Dept. of Justice, etc.) of Public Corruption by named NSA employees. When no action was taken against the NSA employees I researched the Intelligence Community electronic surveillance technology involved and discovered the following publications."

### Sequence:
1. Akwei experiences harassment (1980-1991)
2. He goes to the DOJ about corruption by named individuals (before December 1991)
3. DOJ takes no action
4. He then researches the technology to understand what's being done to him (December 1991 onward)
5. He discovers the bibliography sources
6. He files the lawsuit (February 1992)
7. Case dismissed (March 1992)
8. He compiles the full dossier (1992-1996)
9. Published in Nexus Magazine (April/May 1996)

This is important: he did NOT start with a theory and find evidence. He started with an experience, went to authorities, was ignored, and THEN sought the scientific literature to explain what he was experiencing. The bibliography is post-hoc research, not the foundation of a delusion.

## 10. The "Cover Identity" Claim

Akwei writes: "NSA personnel in the community usually have cover identities such as social workers, lawyers and business owners."

Robert E. Kinnecome's obituary describes him as an NLRB lawyer for 20 years.

Akwei calls him a "retired NSA agent."

Akwei's dossier explicitly lists "lawyers" as a standard NSA cover identity.

Whether Akwei wrote this passage knowing Robert's NLRB title and arguing it was cover, or whether he independently identified "lawyers" as a cover category and Robert's situation fits the pattern, is unknown. But the alignment between Akwei's general claim and Robert's specific situation is exact.
